Markus Meister is a neuroscientist at Caltech (Division of Biology and Biological Engineering, and Chrissy Chen Institute for Neuroscience). Known for work on retinal processing and neural coding, with broader interests in learning and information theory applied to behavior. A consistent theme across his lab is the critique of trial-based laboratory paradigms ([[ethological-paradigms|ethological critique]]) in favor of naturalistic, high-throughput tasks.

## Relevant Work

- [[meister-2022-learning-fast-slow|Meister (2022)]] — review arguing that the 10,000× difference in learning rates across tasks is explained by whether the animal has sparse neural codes for the relevant events, not by task complexity or the speed of synaptic plasticity.
- [[rosenberg-2021-labyrinth-learning|Rosenberg, Zhang, Perona & Meister (2021)]] — empirical complement to the 2022 review: mice in an overnight labyrinth exhibit few-shot reward learning, sudden insight, one-shot home-path learning, and efficient exploration from four consistent local turning biases.
- [[zhang-2024-endotaxis-neuromorphic-navigation|Zhang, Rosenberg, Jing, Perona & Meister (2024)]] — mechanistic proposal: endotaxis, a simple Hebbian circuit that generates "virtual odors" for cognitive navigation by repurposing the ancient chemotaxis controller.
