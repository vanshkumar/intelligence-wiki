Hugh R. Wilson is a theoretical and experimental neuroscientist who, together with [[Jack Cowan]] at the University of Chicago, developed the first general two-variable dynamical description of a cortical population — the **Wilson-Cowan equations** — and subsequently extended them to spatially structured neural fields capable of producing travelling waves and pattern formation. His later career has focused on the psychophysics and computational theory of visual perception, including face processing and spatial vision.

## Selected contributions relevant to this wiki

- [[wilson-cowan-1972-ei-populations|Wilson & Cowan (1972)]] — derivation of the two-variable E-I population equations from refractoriness × sigmoid response × synaptic integration; phase-plane analysis revealing the three generic dynamical regimes (simple hysteresis, multiple hysteresis, limit cycles); Theorem 4 on regime interconvertibility via nonspecific biasing inputs. The template for the neural-mass-modeling literature and the rate-level structured counterpart to [[sompolinsky-1988-chaos-random-networks|SCS (1988)]] chaos theory.
- **Wilson & Cowan (1973)**. Spatial extension of the 1972 model to a 2D neural field `E(x, t), I(x, t)` with local connectivity kernels. Introduced the framework for analyzing pattern formation, travelling waves, and spatial hysteresis in cortical sheets — the ancestor of Ermentrout & Cowan's (1979) hallucination patterns and much of subsequent neural-field theory.
- **Visual psychophysics and cortical computation**. Extensive later work on spatial vision, hyperacuity, and face perception; Wilson's two-stage model of face processing is widely cited in the perception literature.
