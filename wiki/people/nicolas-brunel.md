Nicolas Brunel is a theoretical physicist and computational neuroscientist whose work applies statistical-physics methods — mean-field theory, Fokker-Planck analysis, bifurcation theory — to the dynamics of cortical-like spiking networks. Trained in the Amit lattice-models tradition at École Normale Supérieure, he built much of the modern analytical theory of balanced, sparsely connected excitatory-inhibitory networks of integrate-and-fire neurons.

Selected contributions relevant to this wiki:

- [[brunel-1999-sparsely-connected-networks|Brunel (2000)]] — Comprehensive analytical theory of sparsely connected E-I spiking networks. Identified the four generic regimes (SR, AR, AI, SI) and derived analytic phase diagrams, firing rates, CVs, oscillation frequencies, and finite-size corrections. The spiking-network analogue of [[sompolinsky-1988-chaos-random-networks|SCS (1988)]] and the canonical reference for the balanced-network / asynchronous-irregular framework.
- **Amit & Brunel (1997a, 1997b)** — first self-consistent mean-field treatment of spontaneous and structured activity in recurrently-connected IF networks. Established the bookkeeping (µ, σ, Gaussian-input approximation) that the 2000 paper brings to maturity.
- **Brunel & Hakim (1999)** — analytical treatment of the purely-inhibitory sparse spiking network. Identified the fast-oscillation branch (f ≈ 1/2D) that survives as the fast-SI branch in the full E-I analysis.
- **Later work**: extensive contributions to the theory of cortical attractor networks, working memory, persistent activity, synaptic plasticity, and the statistics of real cortical connectivity (with Lefort, Tolias, and others).
