Computational scientist working at the intersection of statistical machine learning, distributed computing, and the philosophy of how scientific methods should be validated. Earlier appointments at UC Berkeley (EECS, postdoc with Michael I. Jordan) and at MIT (PhD in computational neuroscience, with Joshua Tenenbaum). Now leads a research group at the University of Chicago focused on probabilistic methods for science, including ML methods for chemistry and the use of artificial systems with known ground truth as test beds for the analytical methods of biology and neuroscience.

## Key Contributions in This Wiki

- Co-authored the methodological-philosophical thought experiment ([[jonas-2017-microprocessor-critique|Jonas & Kording, 2017]]) — applied standard systems-neuroscience methods to a fully understood microprocessor (MOS 6502) and showed that the methods produce results superficially similar to neural findings but do not recover computational understanding even when ground truth is known. The paper sharpened "what does it mean to understand a system?" via [[marrs-levels-of-analysis|Marr's levels]] and Lazebnik's "fix it" test, and argued that ground-truth-anchored validation should be a precondition for trusting an analytical method on neural data
- Earlier methodological work on cell-type discovery from connectivity (Jonas & Kording 2015, *eLife*) using stochastic blockmodels — this is one of the methods the 2017 paper itself documents the limits of when applied to a system with a single physical "type"
