Theoretical and computational neuroscientist whose research spans auditory neuroscience (sound localization, pitch perception, spike-timing-based computation), spiking neural network modeling, and the philosophy of neuroscience. Long-running critic of the neural coding metaphor as the organizing framework for neural systems theories. Co-developer of the Brian neural simulator. Maintains an active blog (romainbrette.fr) on conceptual issues in neuroscience.

Brette's program has two halves. The negative half — articulated most fully in [[brette-2018-coding-metaphor|Brette (2018)]] and the earlier *Philosophy of the Spike* (Brette 2015) — argues that the neural coding metaphor is misleading as a basis for theories of brain function: codes have weaker representational power than implied (context-dependence of tuning), they convey only Shannon information by reference to known external messages (which the brain has no access to), and their causal structure is incongruent with the brain's circular, environment-coupled dynamics. The positive half develops alternatives: synchrony receptive fields as representational primitives that can carry sensory laws (Brette 2012; Laudanski 2014; Goodman & Brette 2010); [[subjective-physics|subjective physics]] as a framework for organism-internal information defined as relations among observables and actions (Brette 2016); models of perception that include the full sensorimotor loop rather than chimaeras of encoder + decoder.

For this wiki, Brette is the most explicit philosophical advocate of the framework that the wiki has adopted as its organizing principle: that intelligence begins with closed-loop sensorimotor control, that spikes are *timed actions* rather than symbols, and that perception is the practical mastery of sensorimotor laws rather than the construction of internal pictures.

## Key Contributions in This Wiki

- Sustained philosophical critique of neural coding ([[brette-2018-coding-metaphor|Brette, 2018]]) — the most thorough articulation of why coding-style neural-systems theories fail as theories of brain function. Three-part argument: representational power is weaker than implied, codes carry information by reference rather than by structure, and the linear causal structure of codes is incongruent with the brain's circular causal structure. Companion to [[jonas-2017-microprocessor-critique|Jonas & Kording (2017)]] — methods don't recover Marr-level understanding even with ground truth, and even where they do, the framework that interprets them is incoherent
- The [[subjective-physics|subjective physics]] framework (Brette 2016, expanded in 2018) — information for an organism is the structure of relations among its sensor signals and actions; the organism builds an internal model of these lawful relations. Distinct from generative models (which describe causes-of-inputs) and from Shannon information (which is correspondence to external messages)
- The [[binding-problem|synchrony receptive field]] framework (Brette 2012; Laudanski et al. 2014; Goodman & Brette 2010) — a representational primitive in which a group of neurons whose receptive fields produce equal output defines the *set of stimuli* that elicit synchronous responses, encoding a sensory law (e.g., Jeffress 1948 ITD model: synchrony marks `S_L(t) = S_R(t − d)`). Generalizes binding from feature-conjunction to lawful relations between observables
- Auditory neuroscience contributions on sound localization and pitch perception (Goodman & Brette 2010; Goodman et al. 2013; Laudanski et al. 2014; Benichoux et al. 2015, 2016) — referenced in the 2018 paper as worked examples of why heterogeneous tuning curves and population-level relations are required to resolve the ambiguities single-neuron tuning curves do not
