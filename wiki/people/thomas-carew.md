Thomas Carew is a neurobiologist at NYU's Center for Neural Science. He has been a major contributor for more than four decades to the cellular and molecular study of learning and memory in *Aplysia californica*, working alongside Eric Kandel through the foundational period of the *Aplysia* program and continuing as an independent leader of the field. His lab has been a primary source of evidence that **temporally distributed training produces qualitatively different (longer-lasting, more transcriptionally consolidated) memory than massed training**, and that this difference is decoded at the level of specific kinase cascades.

## Themes in his work relevant to this wiki

- **Spaced vs massed long-term memory in *Aplysia*.** Mauelshagen, Sherff & Carew (1998) — differential induction of long-term synaptic facilitation by spaced vs massed serotonin application; one of the foundational quantitative demonstrations of the spacing effect at the synaptic level. Continued through Philips, Kopec, Carew (2013) on MAPK timing and through Kukushkin et al. 2022 (*PNAS*) on ERK precision timing.
- **Signaling-cascade dynamics as memory mechanism.** Sustained phosphorylation of ERK and CREB after spaced training; positive-feedback loops between CREB and ERK that maintain the consolidated state. The Carew lab's body of work has consistently argued that memory dynamics are properties of cascade kinetics, not properties of the synapse alone.
- **Cellular abstraction of memory.** [[kukushkin-2024-massed-spaced-non-neural|Kukushkin, Carney, Tabassum & Carew (2024)]] — culmination of this thread: the massed-spaced learning effect reproduced in non-neural human cells. Carew is senior author.
- **TGFβ-cascade pattern detection.** Miranda, Mirisis, Asok, Kukushkin & Carew (2023, *PNAS*) extends pattern-detection dynamics to the TGFβ cascade controlling long-term synaptic plasticity in *Aplysia*.

## Position relative to other lineages in this wiki

- **Kandel-era *Aplysia* lineage.** Carew trained with and was closely associated with Eric Kandel during the foundational period of *Aplysia* memory research. The lab's recent work continues that tradition while pushing toward cellular abstraction (cell lines, signaling cascades) rather than circuit-level *Aplysia* preparations.
- **Cellular cognition program.** Through his student Nikolay Kukushkin, Carew is one of the principal voices for [[cellular-cognition|cellular cognition]] — the framework that memory phenomena exist below the neural level, in conserved signaling-cascade dynamics that the nervous system inherited rather than invented.
- **Computational modeling of the spacing effect.** Carew lab work has been a primary empirical input to Smolen-Baxter-Byrne computational models of the spacing effect (2008, 2016) and to Zhang et al. 2011's computational design of enhanced learning protocols.
