Geoffrey Hinton is a pioneer of neural-network research whose work spans Boltzmann machines, backpropagation, the Helmholtz machine, deep belief networks, and the deep-learning revolution of the 2010s. He shared the 2018 ACM Turing Award (with Yoshua Bengio and Yann LeCun) and the 2024 Nobel Prize in Physics (with John Hopfield) for foundational contributions to artificial neural networks. He was co-author on the Helmholtz Machine ([[dayan-hinton-1994-helmholtz-machine|Dayan, Hinton, Neal & Zemel 1994]]) at the University of Toronto.

## Themes in his work relevant to this wiki

- **Boltzmann machines and unsupervised learning.** Early work (Hinton & Sejnowski 1986, Ackley, Hinton & Sejnowski 1985) on stochastic binary networks with symmetric weights and contrastive learning. The Helmholtz machine was an explicit successor that abandoned weight symmetry in favor of a separate recognition network.
- **Backpropagation.** Co-author (with Rumelhart and Williams, 1986) of the modern formulation of backpropagation in multi-layer networks — the algorithm the [[Credit Assignment|dendritic credit-assignment lineage]] aims to approximate biologically.
- **Variational inference in neural networks.** Co-developer of the [[Helmholtz Machine]], introducing variational learning into connectionist architectures years before it became central to modern generative modeling.
- **Deep belief networks and unsupervised pretraining** (Hinton, Osindero & Teh 2006) — stacked restricted Boltzmann machines trained layer-wise; an important successor to the Helmholtz machine that fixed its biased gradients and reignited deep-learning research.
- **Capsule networks, knowledge distillation, contrastive learning** — more recent work on compositional representations and self-supervised learning.
- **Forward-forward algorithm** (Hinton 2022) — a recent proposal for biologically-plausible learning without backpropagation, operating layer-wise via goodness contrasts; thematically continuous with his long interest in avoiding weight transport.
