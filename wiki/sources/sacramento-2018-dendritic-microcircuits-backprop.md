## Overview

This paper proposes that error signals for hierarchical [[Credit Assignment]] are encoded directly in the apical dendrites of cortical pyramidal neurons as **prediction errors** — mismatches between actual top-down feedback from higher areas and predictions of that feedback generated by lateral SST interneurons within each layer. When the lateral microcircuit successfully predicts the top-down input, apical dendrites are silent (the **self-predicting state**). When a novel teaching signal is presented, interneurons cannot explain it away, and the resulting apical voltage deflection propagates to the soma where it modulates the firing rate and drives plasticity at feedforward (basal) synapses.

The key advance over previous biologically plausible learning models is that this operates **continuously in time** — there are no alternating phases (wake/sleep, positive/negative), no clamped/free phases, and all synapses (feedforward, lateral, and top-down) are plastic simultaneously. The paper analytically shows that in the weak-feedback limit (λ → 0), weight updates at feedforward synapses converge to those prescribed by backpropagation.

## Key Findings

### The Neuron and Network Model

Pyramidal neurons are modeled as **three-compartment units**:
- **Basal compartment** (v_B): receives bottom-up (feedforward) input from the layer below
- **Apical compartment** (v_A): receives top-down feedback from the layer above *and* lateral input from same-layer SST interneurons
- **Somatic compartment** (u): integrates basal and apical contributions; drives the output firing rate

The somatic membrane potential evolves as:

**du/dt = −g_lk u + g_B (v_B − u) + g_A (v_A − u) + σξ(t)**

SST interneurons form a second population at each layer. They receive lateral projections from same-layer pyramidal cells (driving their basal dendrite) and a weak cross-layer "nudging" signal from pyramidal neurons one layer up (driving their soma toward the upper-layer activity). Interneurons project back to pyramidal apical dendrites.

### The Self-Predicting State

The lateral microcircuit — pyramidal-to-interneuron synapses (W^IP) and interneuron-to-pyramidal apical synapses (W^PI) — learns to predict and cancel the top-down feedback. Once converged:

1. Interneurons reproduce the activity of the layer above (their lateral weights learn to implement the same function as the top-down weights)
2. Interneuron-to-pyramidal synapses cancel the top-down input at the apical dendrite
3. Apical compartments fall silent (v_A → 0)

In this state, the network behaves as if it were a purely feedforward network — top-down and lateral contributions annihilate each other. The somatic potential is driven only by the basal (bottom-up) prediction.

### Error Propagation from Self-Predictions

When a novel teaching signal arrives at the output layer (via somatic nudging of output neurons toward a target), prediction errors appear:

1. Output layer pyramidal neurons are pushed toward the target → their activity changes
2. Layer below: top-down feedback now carries the new target-influenced activity, but lateral interneurons still predict the old activity → **mismatch at apical dendrites** = prediction error
3. This apical error propagates to the soma, modulating the firing rate
4. The changed firing rate creates a new mismatch one layer further down
5. Errors cascade backward through the network

The apical compartment voltage at hidden layer k encodes, to first order:

**v_A,k ∝ λ^(N−k+1) · (product of D_l W_{l,l+1}) · D_N · (u_N^tgt − u_N)**

This is the backpropagated error: the output error (u_N^tgt − u_N), multiplied by the chain of derivative matrices and weight matrices — exactly the structure of the backpropagation algorithm.

### Synaptic Learning Rules

All plasticity rules belong to the **dendritic predictive plasticity** family (Urbanczik & Senn 2014):

**dw/dt = η [φ(u) − φ(v)] r**

where u and v are compartmental potentials and r is presynaptic input. The weight changes according to the product of a **dendritic prediction error** (mismatch between somatic and dendritic activity) and presynaptic rate.

Three connection types, three rules:
- **Feedforward (W^PP)**: error = somatic rate − basal prediction → learns bottom-up associations (Eq. 7)
- **Lateral pyramidal-to-interneuron (W^IP)**: error = somatic rate − basal prediction of interneuron → interneurons learn to mirror upper-layer activity (Eq. 8)
- **Interneuron-to-pyramidal apical (W^PI)**: drives apical voltage toward rest → interneurons learn to cancel top-down input (Eq. 9)

All rules are **local** — each synapse sees only its own pre- and postsynaptic compartmental potentials.

### Analytical Equivalence to Backpropagation

In the weak-feedback limit (λ → 0, where λ controls the strength of top-down relative to bottom-up drive):

**ΔW_{k,k−1} = η λ^(N−k+1) · (∏ D_l W_{l,l+1}) · D_N · (u_N^tgt − u_N) · r_{k−1}^T + O(λ^(N−k+2))**

This matches the backpropagation weight update **ΔW^bp** up to a factor of λ^(N−k), provided top-down weights equal the transpose of feedforward weights (or feedback alignment holds). The self-predicting microcircuit implicitly computes and transmits backpropagated errors without any explicit backward pass.

### Feedback Alignment Emerges Naturally

Top-down weights need not be precisely symmetric with feedforward weights. When both are plastic, feedforward weights spontaneously align to ~45° of the (fixed random) feedback weights during learning — the **feedback alignment** phenomenon (Lillicrap et al. 2016). When top-down weights are also learned (via Eq. 10), the network implements a form of **target propagation** (inverse reconstruction), and alignment improves further. Either way, weight symmetry is not required.

### Experimental Results

**Nonlinear regression** (Fig. 2): A 30-50-10 pyramidal neuron network learns to approximate a random nonlinear function from input-output pairs, significantly outperforming a shallow learner. The self-predicting state emerges and is maintained throughout learning.

**MNIST classification** (Fig. 3): A 784-500-500-10 four-layer network achieves **1.96% test error** (vs. 1.53% for standard backprop). Performance is comparable to feedback alignment (~1.6–2.4%) and favorably compares with Guerguiev et al. (2017)'s 3.28% for a two-hidden-layer dendritic network.

**Deeper networks**: Fixing the first hidden layer's weights and learning only the second hidden layer still achieves 2.11%, confirming that errors propagate usefully through multiple layers.

**Learned top-down weights**: With plastic top-down connections, a 784-1000-10 network achieves 2.48% — the microcircuit tracks changes in both bottom-up and top-down pathways simultaneously.

## Methods

- **Neuron model**: Three-compartment conductance-based pyramidal neurons (soma, basal, apical) + two-compartment SST interneurons
- **Dynamics**: Continuous-time leaky integration with Gaussian noise; soft rectifying nonlinearity φ(u) = γ log(1 + exp(β(u − θ))) in sparse firing regime
- **Training**: Online, continuous-time — no mini-batches or alternating phases. Patterns presented sequentially with smooth transitions. Weight changes low-pass filtered.
- **Approximate dynamics**: For MNIST, simplified to fixed-point iteration (alternating forward/backward sweeps) for computational efficiency, with convex combination updates

## Implications

### Unifying Predictive Coding and Credit Assignment

This paper provides the most direct bridge between [[Predictive Coding]] and [[Credit Assignment]] in the wiki. The prediction error *is* the credit signal: the apical dendrite compares what higher areas are saying (top-down feedback) with what the local microcircuit expected them to say (lateral interneuron prediction). When the comparison fails, the resulting error is precisely the backpropagated gradient. This means predictive coding and backpropagation are not competing theories of cortical learning — they are two descriptions of the same dendritic microcircuit operating at different levels.

### The Self-Predicting State as Default

The network's equilibrium is a self-predicting state where all errors are canceled. Learning is triggered by **perturbation** from this equilibrium — a novel teaching signal at the output creates a cascade of prediction errors that propagate backward. This is consistent with the control thesis: the system maintains a homeostatic equilibrium (all predictions satisfied = no action needed), and deviations from equilibrium drive corrective updates.

### Position in the Credit Assignment Lineage

Sacramento (2018) occupies a specific position between [[kording-konig-2001-two-integration-sites|Kording & Konig (2001)]] and [[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al. (2020)]]:

| Feature | Kording & Konig (2001) | Guerguiev et al. (2017) | Sacramento et al. (2018) | Payeur et al. (2020) |
|---------|----------------------|------------------------|------------------------|---------------------|
| Neuron model | Rate, two variables | **Spiking**, three compartments | Rate, three compartments | Spiking, two compartments |
| Error representation | Burst rate D | Plateau potential difference | Apical voltage (prediction error) | Burst probability deviation (B − P̄) |
| Interneuron role | Not modeled | Not modeled | Predict & cancel top-down input | Linearize burst probability |
| Learning phases | Not addressed | **Yes** (forward/plateau) | **None** (continuous) | None (continuous) |
| Feedback alignment | Assumes symmetric | Emerges during training | Emerges naturally; also learned TD | Learned via burst-dependent rule |
| Predictive coding link | Implicit | Implicit (two-phase ≈ predict/compare) | Explicit — errors *are* prediction errors | Implicit (P̄ as prediction) |
| Scale | X-OR | MNIST (3.28%) | MNIST (1.96%) | CIFAR-10, ImageNet |
| Self-supervised variant | Yes (Imax) | No | No | No |

Sacramento's key advance over [[guerguiev-2017-segregated-dendrites|Guerguiev et al. (2017)]] is eliminating the phase requirement by introducing SST interneurons that continuously predict top-down feedback — the self-predicting state replaces the forward/plateau alternation. Both share the three-compartment architecture and feedback alignment, but Sacramento operates continuously with no timing coordination needed.

Sacramento shares the two-site architecture from Kording but operationalizes the apical compartment differently — as a site for **prediction error computation** rather than a direct carrier of the burst-rate learning signal. Payeur then adds biophysical fidelity (spiking, VGCCs, calcium spikes) and scales further, but drops the explicit predictive coding interpretation.

### Experimental Predictions

The paper makes several testable predictions:
1. **Distal dendrites encode error signals** — prediction errors should be detectable as voltage deflections in apical dendrites when top-down feedback is inconsistent with lateral predictions. Supported by Zmarz & Keller (2016) and Attinger et al. (2017) in mouse visual cortex.
2. **Prediction errors co-occur across hierarchical levels** — if errors propagate backward, prediction signals at higher cortical areas should predict errors at earlier areas. Schwiedrzik & Freiwald (2017) observe this in macaque face-processing hierarchy.
3. **Apical-targeting inhibitory plasticity is central** — the rule for interneuron-to-pyramidal apical synapses (Eq. 9) is a dendritic variant of homeostatic inhibitory plasticity (Vogels et al. 2011). Disrupting this should prevent the self-predicting state from forming and impair learning. Supported by Chiu et al. (2018).

## Connections to Existing Knowledge

- **[[Credit Assignment]]**: Provides a predictive-coding-based mechanism for structural credit assignment that is analytically equivalent to backpropagation. Sits between [[kording-konig-2001-two-integration-sites|Kording & Konig (2001)]] (conceptual seed) and [[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al. (2020)]] (spiking implementation) in the lineage.
- **[[Predictive Coding]]**: Makes the connection between predictive coding and credit assignment explicit — the prediction error at apical dendrites *is* the backpropagated error signal. This is the strongest link between the two frameworks in the wiki.
- **[[Dendritic Computation]]**: Adds a third functional interpretation of the apical dendrite: beyond carrying a learning signal ([[kording-konig-2001-two-integration-sites|Kording & Konig]]) or controlling burst probability ([[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al.]]), the apical compartment computes a **prediction error** via comparison of top-down and lateral inputs.
- **[[Hebbian Learning]]**: The plasticity rules (Eqs. 7-9) are all local — products of dendritic prediction errors and presynaptic rates. This extends the "Hebbian but enriched by architecture" theme.
- **[[Inhibitory Circuits]]**: SST interneurons here serve a specific computational role: learning to predict and cancel top-down feedback, establishing the self-predicting baseline from which errors are measured. This is distinct from their role in Payeur (linearization) and adds a new facet to the SST+ functional story.

## Open Questions Raised

- Can the self-predicting state be detected experimentally? If apical dendrites are silent when the network has converged and noisy when learning, this should be measurable with dendritic calcium imaging during task acquisition vs. post-learning. *Partially addressed by [[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni et al. (2026)]]*: SD residuals during learning are cell-specific and task-relevant, consistent with dendritic error signals; P- neuron activity decreases over 14 days, consistent with movement toward a quieter state, but full convergence to silence was not observed.
- How does the model handle temporal credit assignment? Like Payeur (2020), this addresses only spatial (structural) credit assignment — errors propagate across layers for immediate targets. Sequences and delayed rewards are not addressed.
- The model requires one SST interneuron per pyramidal neuron (one-to-one connectivity). How sensitive is the mechanism to violations of this constraint? Recent monosynaptic input mapping (Leinweber et al. 2017) supports approximately one-to-one SST→pyramidal projections, but exact one-to-one is unlikely.
- Can the continuous-time, no-phases property be preserved when moving to spiking neurons? Payeur et al. (2020) operate in continuous time but use a burst/spike distinction rather than continuous apical voltage — does the predictive coding interpretation survive in the spiking regime?
- The weak-feedback limit (λ → 0) is needed for the analytical equivalence to backprop, but the simulations use λ ≈ 0.1–0.3. How much does the approximation degrade at biologically realistic feedback strengths?
- How does this interact with [[Memory Consolidation|sleep-dependent consolidation]]? The authors note that neocortex and hippocampus could play complementary roles (McClelland et al. 1995) — the microcircuit learns slowly from many examples (like neocortex), while one-shot learning might require a hippocampal system.
