## Overview

The Introduction to [[Max Bennett]]'s book lays out a methodological argument: the brain (86 billion neurons, trillions of connections, hundreds of neurotransmitters, constantly changing wiring) is too complex to reverse-engineer directly. Instead, tracing evolutionary history provides an **ordered decomposition** from simple to complex. Ancestral brains can be reconstructed through fossils and by comparing modern animal brains, which serve as "time capsules." The book is organized around five major breakthroughs in brain evolution.

## Key Findings

### Evolutionary Decomposition as Methodology

The core argument: rather than trying to understand the human brain all at once, trace the evolutionary sequence from simple nervous systems to complex ones. Each step is smaller and more tractable. Modern animals with simpler nervous systems serve as approximate models of ancestral stages. AI serves as a bidirectional litmus test (Feynman principle: "What I cannot create, I do not understand").

### The Triune Brain Hypothesis Is Wrong

MacLean's model of three stacked layers (reptilian brain for instincts, limbic system for emotions, neocortex for cognition) fails on four counts:
1. **Wrong anatomy** — reptiles have limbic structures
2. **Wrong functional divisions** — cognition, emotion, and instinct span all brain regions
3. **Wrong evolutionary story** — evolution modifies existing systems, doesn't just stack new layers on top
4. **Too vague** — the categories are not specific enough to be scientifically useful

### The Five Breakthroughs Framework

The book organizes brain evolution as five major breakthroughs, each building on the last, each driven by extreme environmental pressures and predator-prey feedback loops. One part of the book per breakthrough. (The specific breakthroughs are enumerated in later chapters.)

### Scala Naturae Warning

All extant species have been evolving equally long — there is no hierarchy from "simple" to "complex." Cognitive abilities like episodic memory may have evolved independently in multiple lineages (convergent evolution), just as wings evolved independently in insects, bats, and birds. The only ranking evolution recognizes is survived vs. did not survive.

## Implications

The methodological framing matters for the wiki: understanding the brain's learning mechanisms benefits from knowing the evolutionary order in which they emerged. Simpler mechanisms (reflexes, habituation, basic associative learning) preceded more complex ones (spatial maps, episodic memory, abstract reasoning), and each layer of complexity builds on and is constrained by the previous one.

## Connections to Existing Knowledge

- General framing for the wiki's evolutionary perspective on learning
- The rejection of the triune brain model is important context: there is no clean separation between "old" and "new" brain systems for learning
