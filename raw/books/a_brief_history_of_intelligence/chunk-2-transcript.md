**Claude**

The fungi explanation is solid. Let me correct the three levels though — they're not organized by kingdom, they're organized by size and mobility:

1. Single-celled life (bacteria and single-celled eukaryotes)
2. Small multicellular life — big enough to engulf single-celled organisms, small enough to still move using cellular propellers
3. Large multicellular life — too big for cellular propellers, therefore immobile

The important thing is that level 3 is defined by an engineering constraint: past a certain size, cellular propellers can't move you anymore, so you're stuck in place. Plants, fungi, and early animals all hit this level.

On fungi vs. animals — you got the core right. One thing to add: Bennett draws a specific contrast in *feeding mechanism*. Fungi use external digestion (secrete enzymes outside the body, absorb nutrients). Animals use internal digestion (trap food inside the body, then break it down). The animal strategy — actively catching and ingesting prey — is the one that demands sensing, moving, and coordinating, which is what neurons are for.

This connects back to the theme you just identified: fungi took the easier path (wait for things to die), animals took the harder path (actively hunt), and the harder path drove the innovation of neurons.
