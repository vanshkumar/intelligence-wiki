# Open Questions

Research gaps, unresolved debates, and questions to guide future source selection. Updated as sources are ingested — questions get added when a paper raises them, and marked resolved (with links) when answered.

These questions are organized by level but unified by the wiki's organizing principle: intelligence begins with closed-loop sensorimotor control, and learning/memory extend that control over longer timescales, more latent variables, and more counterfactual worlds.

---

## Control & Sensorimotor Foundations

- What is the minimal closed-loop sensorimotor system that exhibits learning? [[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|Li et al. (2024)]] shows prediction + noise suffices for reward-seeking in a toy network — but what is the simplest *biological* system where this can be tested?
- How does the transition from reactive control (immediate sensorimotor loop) to model-based control (planning, counterfactual reasoning) happen across evolution and development? Is it a continuum or are there discrete architectural innovations?
- If genes encode behavior by constraining neurons' nonlinear feedback control algorithms (rather than specifying circuits), what are the specific molecular-to-dynamical mappings? Ion channel expression → membrane dynamics → network-level attractor structure is the rough chain, but the intermediate steps are poorly characterized.
- The [[Degeneracy|degeneracy]] observation — conserved macroscopic dynamics despite variable microscopic configurations ([[brennan-2019-conserved-macroscopic-dynamics|Brennan & Proekt, 2019]]) — is predicted by a control-theoretic view of genes. Can this be made quantitative: given a distribution over neuronal parameters set by gene expression, what is the probability of the target macroscopic structure emerging?
- How do memory systems (hippocampal consolidation, cortical sparse codes) feed back into the sensorimotor loop? Is the right framing that memory *serves* control — building internal models that allow the loop to operate over longer horizons and more latent variables?

## Molecular & Cellular

- What are the fundamental molecular mechanisms of memory formation and storage?
- How do synaptic and non-synaptic plasticity mechanisms interact?
- What role does protein synthesis play in memory consolidation, and what triggers it?
- How exactly is calcium involved in synaptic plasticity? What are the specific molecular cascades from calcium influx to lasting synaptic change, and what determines whether a calcium signal produces LTP vs. LTD?
- Where does the membrane potential as an electrical circuit analogy break down? What phenomena require going beyond the RC circuit / Hodgkin-Huxley framework?
- What is the space of nonlinear dynamical systems that a single neuron can implement using its complement of ion channels? How much computational richness is available at the single-cell level before network effects?
  - *Partially addressed*: [[jones-2020-dendritic-computation-power|Jones & Kording (2020)]] show that dendritic branching with nonlinearities gives a single neuron the power of a multi-layer network (approaching a 2-layer FCNN). But this uses an abstract model (leaky ReLU); the full space of dynamics from biophysical ion channels in dendrites remains unexplored.

## Circuits & Systems

- How do different brain regions coordinate during learning?
- What determines whether a memory is consolidated into long-term storage or forgotten?
  - *Partially addressed*: [[wierzynski-2009-sleep-hpc-pfc|Wierzynski et al. (2009)]] show that SWR-driven hippocampus→PFC spike timing during SWS falls within the STDP window, providing a candidate mechanism. But what determines which memories get replayed (and thus consolidated) vs. which are lost remains unknown.
- How does the hippocampus interact with cortical regions during systems consolidation?
  - *Partially addressed*: [[wierzynski-2009-sleep-hpc-pfc|Wierzynski et al. (2009)]] demonstrate directional CA1→mPFC spike timing during SWS, driven by SWR events. This is direct single-cell-pair evidence for hippocampus-to-cortex communication during sleep. However, whether this timing actually induces plasticity (vs. merely falling in the right window) has not been shown causally.
- Is the travelling wave property of [[Theta Oscillations|hippocampal theta]] **causally necessary** for spatial behavior, or would synchronized theta work just as well? ([[lubenov-2009-theta-travelling-waves|Lubenov & Siapas, 2009]] — causal experiment not yet done)
- What is the computational advantage of segment over point spatial encoding in the [[Hippocampus]]? Does it support planning, prediction, or multi-scale spatial reasoning?
- When should travelling-wave structure in neural oscillations be interpreted as part of the computation vs. a geometric side effect of coordinated activity?

## Development & Plasticity

- What opens and closes critical periods for learning?
- How does adult neurogenesis contribute to learning and memory?
- How does the brain maintain stability while remaining plastic (the stability-plasticity dilemma)?

## Representations & Learning Speed
*(New — added from Meister 2022)*

- Does passive exposure to 2AFC task stimuli (without reinforcement) build [[Sparse Coding|sparse codes]] that accelerate subsequent reinforced learning? ([[meister-2022-learning-fast-slow|Meister, 2022]] — proposed but untested). [[reinert-2021-pfc-categorization|Reinert et al. (2021)]] show gradual emergence of category selectivity in mPFC during reinforced training, but never separate passive exposure from reinforced learning — the specific passive pre-exposure experiment remains undone.
- What are the specific unsupervised learning mechanisms that build sparse codes in cortex? How do they relate to [[Predictive Coding]]?
- How do neuromodulatory systems (dopamine, norepinephrine) interact with the sparse code framework — do they modulate which codes get built, which associations form on existing codes, or both?
- Are lab 2AFC tasks studying the brain's learning machinery, or mostly studying representation formation — a process that may be irrelevant to how the animal actually learns in the wild?
- Is the gradual emergence of category selectivity in [[Prefrontal Cortex|mPFC]] driven by slow building of upstream sensory representations, slow rule learning in PFC itself, or both? Simultaneous imaging of sensory cortex and PFC during learning would be needed to distinguish these. ([[reinert-2021-pfc-categorization|Reinert et al., 2021]])
- Why do Go-preferring and NoGo-preferring PFC neurons show asymmetric dynamics during rule switching (remapping vs replacement)? What determines which mechanism a neuron uses?

## Neural Dynamics & Manifolds
*(New — added from Brennan & Proekt 2019)*

- Does the [[Neural Manifolds|manifold]] framework scale to organisms with more complex nervous systems? Higher-dimensional dynamics may require fundamentally different extraction methods. ([[brennan-2019-conserved-macroscopic-dynamics|Brennan & Proekt, 2019]])
- What is the biological substrate of the conserved manifold? If individual neuron activations differ but dynamics are conserved, what physical properties enforce this constraint — is it connectivity topology, neuromodulatory state, or something else?
- How does the manifold change during learning? Does acquiring a new behavior correspond to a topological change (new loop) or a parametric change (modified flow)?
- What determines which neurons are informative for manifold reconstruction? Is there a principled way to identify the ~5% that suffice, or must it be done empirically?
- Does the 1D trajectory approximation (LOOPER) hold for open-ended tasks involving continuous interaction with changing environments, or only for trial-based classification? ([[brennan-2023-looper-computational-scaffold|Brennan et al., 2023]])
- Why do biological systems consistently find general computational strategies while RNNs trained on the same tasks exploit dataset-specific statistics? Is this a consequence of evolutionary selection pressure for generalization, or a structural property of biological networks? ([[brennan-2023-looper-computational-scaffold|Brennan et al., 2023]])
- Can computational scaffolds be compared formally across systems to quantify computational similarity — not just "same number of branches" but a rigorous distance metric?

## Computation & Theory

- What is the brain's "learning algorithm"? How does it compare to gradient descent and other artificial learning rules?
  - *Partially addressed*: [[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|Li et al. (2024)]] shows local prediction + noise is sufficient for reward-seeking, suggesting the "algorithm" may be simpler than expected — but only demonstrated in toy environments.
  - *Partially addressed*: [[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento et al. (2018)]] show that dendritic predictive plasticity rules (local compartmental mismatch × presynaptic rate) are analytically equivalent to backpropagation in the weak-feedback limit. The "algorithm" may be local prediction error minimization implemented by dendritic architecture.
- Does the brain implement something like predictive coding or free energy minimization?
  - *Partially addressed*: [[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|Li et al. (2024)]] shows predictive coding can drive behavior (not just perception) when combined with noise, but the bottom-up prediction direction in PaN differs from classic top-down formulations.
  - *Substantially addressed*: [[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento et al. (2018)]] show that predictive coding and backpropagation are not competing theories — they are two descriptions of the same dendritic microcircuit. The apical dendrite computes a prediction error (top-down feedback minus lateral interneuron prediction), and this error is the backpropagated gradient. The remaining question is whether this holds in spiking networks at scale.
- How does the brain perform credit assignment across multiple synapses and time steps?
  - *Partially addressed*: [[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento et al. (2018)]] show that dendritic prediction errors at apical dendrites encode backpropagated gradients, with analytical proof in the weak-feedback limit. Continuous-time operation (no phases), 1.96% MNIST. [[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al. (2020)]] bring this to spiking biophysical fidelity and scale to CIFAR-10. Both address **structural** (spatial) credit assignment across layers. However, **temporal** credit assignment (delayed rewards, sequences) remains unaddressed — both mechanisms only handle immediate error signals.
  - *Flagged as limitation*: PaN has no mechanism for temporal credit assignment — only handles immediate sensory feedback ([[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|Li et al. (2024)]]).
- What does it mean to "understand the brain"? What would a satisfying explanation look like — a predictive model, a compression of experimental data, an engineered system that reproduces behavior, or something else? How do we know when we've understood something vs. merely described it?
- What mathematical language is appropriate for describing brain computation? Is it dynamical systems, information theory, statistical mechanics, category theory, or something else — and does the answer depend on the level of description (single neuron vs. circuit vs. system)?
- What might a useful analog to Kolmogorov complexity look like in the brain? Is there a meaningful sense in which some neural representations or computations are "simpler" than others, and does the brain preferentially find low-complexity solutions?
- How does dopamine as reward prediction error solve temporal credit assignment? The RPE signal is global and scalar — how does the brain route it to the specific synapses that were causally responsible for the outcome, potentially many seconds earlier?
- Can universality class / distribution results from random matrix theory help us understand neural population dynamics? Do eigenvalue spectra of neural correlation matrices fall into known universality classes, and if so, what does that imply about the underlying dynamics?

## Evolution & Multi-Scale Organization

- How does DNA bridge the micro (local neuronal learning rules) to the macro (evolutionarily relevant behavior)? DNA encodes proteins, not circuits or behaviors — what are the intermediate layers of organization, and how constrained is the mapping from genome to neural architecture to behavioral repertoire?

## Dendritic Computation & Single-Neuron Power
*(New — added from Jones & Kording 2020)*

- What is the computational role of dendritic morphological diversity? Different neuron types have radically different dendritic trees — does this correspond to different computational specializations?
- How does the *k*-tree (raw computational power) framework interact with dendritic compartmentalization (Payeur, credit assignment)? Can a single neuron simultaneously exploit both multi-layer computation within branches AND feedforward/feedback separation across compartments?
- Does the brain optimize dendritic morphology during development for expected input statistics? Is branching pattern learned, genetically determined, or both?
- How does biologically structured sparsity (dendritic tree topology) compare to other sparsity patterns (random, pruned, lottery ticket) in computational efficiency?

## Hierarchical Learning & Credit Assignment
*(Updated with Greedy et al. 2022 — BurstCCN synthesis)*

- Does the burst-dependent credit assignment mechanism extend to **temporal** credit assignment (sequences, delayed rewards), or only spatial credit assignment across layers? ([[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al. (2020)]])
- Does a developmental period of "learning to do credit assignment" exist in mammalian cortex? [[guerguiev-2017-segregated-dendrites|Guerguiev et al. (2017)]] show feedback alignment converging over training epochs (weight update angle to backprop: ~65° → ~40°). This might correspond to critical period plasticity — an early period where the cortical hierarchy calibrates its feedback pathways before effective hierarchical learning can begin.
- What biological mechanism coordinates the forward and plateau phases that [[guerguiev-2017-segregated-dendrites|Guerguiev et al. (2017)]] require? Candidates include neuromodulatory gating (e.g., cholinergic bursts), oscillatory phase structuring (beta/gamma cycles), and SST interneuron disinhibition. [[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento et al. (2018)]] eliminate the phase requirement entirely via the self-predicting state — but if phases do exist in cortex, the question of their coordination mechanism remains.
- Can the self-predicting state ([[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento et al. 2018]]) be detected experimentally? If apical dendrites are silent post-learning and noisy during learning, this should be measurable with dendritic calcium imaging. Zmarz & Keller (2016) and Attinger et al. (2017) provide initial support in mouse visual cortex, but the longitudinal trajectory (noisy → silent as learning converges) has not been demonstrated.
  - *Partially addressed*: [[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni et al. (2026)]] show that SD residuals (dendritic activity not predicted by somatic activity) contain task-relevant information during learning and are cell-specific. The 14-day trajectory shows P+ neurons maintaining activity while P- neurons decrease — consistent with selective error-driven shaping — but full convergence to a silent self-predicting state was not observed within the training window.
- Does the predictive-coding / credit-assignment unification ([[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento et al. 2018]]) survive in spiking networks? The paper uses rate neurons; [[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al. (2020)]] use spiking neurons but with a burst-probability rather than prediction-error interpretation. Bridging these — spiking neurons with explicit dendritic prediction errors — would close the lineage.
  - *Partially addressed experimentally*: [[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni et al. (2026)]] show that dendritic signals encode error derivatives (more consistent with target propagation) rather than prediction errors per se. GCaMP imaging cannot distinguish burst vs. isolated spike contributions, so whether the SD residual reflects burst-rate modulation (as the computational lineage predicts) or a different dendritic signal type remains open.
- How sensitive is the Sacramento microcircuit to violations of one-to-one SST-to-pyramidal connectivity? The model assumes one interneuron per pyramidal neuron. Leinweber et al. (2017) show approximately targeted SST projections, but exact one-to-one is implausible. Does the mechanism degrade gracefully?
- Can the self-supervised variant of two-site learning ([[kording-konig-2001-two-integration-sites|Kording & Konig 2001]]'s Imax-style coherence-across-streams rule) be implemented with the full spiking/STP machinery of Payeur et al.? The 2001 paper proves the principle; the modern spiking implementation remains undone. This matters because most biological learning is unsupervised.
- What is the actual transfer function D = g(apical input, A) for dendritic calcium spike generation in vivo? The backprop implementation requires D ∝ A(1 - A) (sigmoid derivative), which is a strong biophysical assumption. The shape of the BAC firing response curve — particularly its saturating behavior — is incompletely characterized. ([[kording-konig-2001-two-integration-sites|Kording & Konig, 2001]])
- How does burst-dependent plasticity interact with neuromodulatory systems (dopamine, norepinephrine)? The model abstracts all neuromodulation into a gating term M(t) — what fills that role biologically?
- Can the mechanism support unsupervised learning, or does it require a supervised teaching signal? Could bursting + NMDA cooperativity + feedforward inhibition be adapted for unsupervised sequence learning?
- Does the STP polarization prediction hold across cortical areas — STD at feedforward synapses, STF at feedback synapses along the sensory hierarchy? Current evidence is mixed. ([[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al. (2020)]]). [[greedy-2022-single-phase-burstccn|Greedy et al. (2022)]] sharpen this prediction: the model requires **two** types of feedback connection with different STP (STF via interneurons, STD direct) converging on the same apical dendrites. Is this level of STP specificity actually present?
- BurstCCN with random feedback weights performs significantly worse than with W-Y symmetric weights on CIFAR-10 (~39% vs ~23%). Can biologically plausible feedback learning rules close this gap? This is the main remaining performance limitation of the dendritic credit assignment lineage. ([[greedy-2022-single-phase-burstccn|Greedy et al. (2022)]])
- Can any model in the lineage be extended to **temporal** credit assignment (sequences, delayed rewards)? All five computational papers (Kording → Guerguiev → Sacramento → Payeur → Greedy) address only spatial (structural) credit assignment. Recurrent connections and temporal error signals are unaddressed. [[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni et al. (2026)]] validates spatial credit assignment experimentally but does not test temporal credit.
- Does the vectorized dendritic teaching signal ([[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni et al. (2026)]]) generalize beyond neurofeedback BCI to naturalistic learning? BCI artificially defines the neuron-to-outcome mapping — does the same cell-specific SD residual structure exist during sensory learning, motor learning, or navigation where causal roles are not experimenter-imposed?
- How do NDNF+ and SST+ interneurons interact in implementing credit assignment? The computational lineage assigns linearization and prediction roles to SST+; Francioni assigns a gating role to NDNF+ at L1. A dual-perturbation experiment (SST+ vs NDNF+ vs both) would disambiguate their contributions and test the proposed laminar division of labor.
- Can the SD residual be decomposed into burst-rate vs. isolated-spike contributions using voltage imaging or electrophysiology? GCaMP cannot distinguish these; the computational lineage specifically predicts burst-rate modulation as the credit signal carrier.

## Sleep & Memory Consolidation
*(New — added from Wierzynski et al. 2009)*

- Does the SWR-driven hippocampus→PFC spike timing during SWS **actually induce synaptic plasticity**, or does it merely fall within the STDP window without producing lasting change? Causal evidence (e.g., blocking SWR replay and measuring consolidation deficit) is needed. ([[wierzynski-2009-sleep-hpc-pfc|Wierzynski et al., 2009]])
- What information is carried in SWR-driven hippocampal replay? Is it a faithful reinstatement of waking experience, a compressed/edited version, or a generative recombination? This determines whether consolidation is "copying" or "distilling."
- What explains the biphasic prefrontal response to hippocampal input — early excitation (0-100 ms) followed by late inhibition (100-200 ms)? Is the inhibitory phase a gating signal, a reset mechanism, or something else?
- Does hippocampal-prefrontal coupling strength change with learning? If SWR-driven consolidation builds cortical representations gradually ([[reinert-2021-pfc-categorization|Reinert et al., 2021]]), coupling should be stronger during active learning and weaker once consolidation is complete.
- What is the functional role of REM sleep in the two-stage model? Wierzynski shows hippocampal-prefrontal coupling is abolished during REM — but does REM perform cortical-internal reorganization (strengthening, pruning, integrating), and how?
- The 3 cell pairs with REM-correlated timing (out of 90) may be noise or may indicate a small subpopulation with a distinct consolidation role during REM. Is there a REM-specific hippocampal-cortical pathway?

## Cognitive Navigation & Endotaxis
*(New — added from Zhang et al. 2024)*

- Is [[Endotaxis]] actually the computation used in the hippocampus? The goal-cell signature (very large place field, sudden single-event emergence) is testable with current imaging. Are the three predicted cell populations (point, map, goal) dissociable in recordings?
- How is the mode switch — which selects among goal signals (food, water, home, neglect) and routes the chosen one to the chemotaxis module — implemented biologically? [[zhang-2024-endotaxis-neuromorphic-navigation|Zhang et al. (2024)]] treats it as given; circuit tracing to a module receiving convergent goal inputs and projecting to the taxis controller is the experimental entry point.
- Can endotaxis be extended to continuous (non-graph) environments? Point cells with overlapping receptive fields should generalize, but the monotonicity theorem is proved only for discrete graphs.
- The critical gain γ_c depends on graph topology via λ_max(A). Can neurons tune γ automatically based on environmental connectivity statistics, so the same circuit works across structurally different environments?
- Endotaxis is trial-and-error at each decision. How does it compose with sequence-level planning (chaining actions into motifs, internal forward models as in Kay et al. 2020 / Nyberg et al. 2022)? Is there a natural extension of the endotaxis circuit that supports prospective rollout?
- Is the [[Mushroom Body]] → [[Hippocampus]] motif correspondence **deep homology** (pre-bilaterian origin) or **convergent evolution**? Either way, what does the shared motif tell us about the optimality landscape of associative-learning architectures?
- The formal overlap between endotaxis and the [[Successor Representation]] suggests a single underlying "resolvent-of-connectivity" primitive that can be read out as value function or as goal gradient depending on downstream circuits. Is there biological evidence for both readouts coexisting?
- Does endotaxis interact with [[Memory Consolidation]] — is the learned map subsequently consolidated to cortical long-term storage, and if so, how does the goal-signal machinery persist or transfer?
- Bittner et al. (2017) observed sudden CA1 place-field formation via dendritic calcium plateaus over seconds-long windows. Is this the mechanism by which endotaxis goal cells write their synapses? The timescale of the plasticity rule endotaxis needs matches behavioral-time-scale plasticity observed experimentally.

## Ethology, Exploration & Sudden Insight
*(New — added from Rosenberg et al. 2021)*

- What is the neural substrate of the four local turning biases ([[rosenberg-2021-labyrinth-learning|Rosenberg et al. 2021]])? Are they implemented in a dedicated navigation circuit (entorhinal-hippocampal) or in a more general action-selection structure?
- What is the mechanism of **sudden insight**? Candidate accounts — attractor phase transitions, representational switching via sparse-code emergence, consolidation-driven reorganization, control-hierarchy threshold crossings — are not mutually exclusive, and none are yet experimentally distinguished.
- Can the cross-animal consistency of turning biases (SD ~0.03 across 19 mice) be traced to specific genes or developmental parameters? What is the control-theoretic mapping from gene expression to these four bias parameters? This is the sharpest available test of the genes→control→structure hypothesis.
- Do inbred vs. outbred mouse strains differ in the four bias parameters? Outbred strains would directly test how tightly the biases are genetically constrained.
- Why does exploration remain the dominant behavioral mode (~75–95%) even in rewarded animals after learning? Is it hedging, intrinsic reward, or that reward simply does not suppress the default mode?
- Is one-shot home-path learning dependent on hippocampal [[Place Cells|place cell]] formation during the inbound traversal, or does it rely primarily on path-integration machinery? Rotation-robust navigation favors the former but does not distinguish them.
- Does intrinsic exploration have a neuromodulatory signature (locus coeruleus / norepinephrine tone)? This connects the [[Explore-Exploit Tradeoff]] mechanism to the observed dominance of exploration in [[intrinsic-exploration|intrinsic exploration]].

## Computation Through Dynamics & Motor Cortex
*(New — added from Vyas et al. 2020)*

- What determines the intrinsic neural manifold? Is the ~10D subspace containing motor-cortex population variance primarily shaped by anatomical connectivity, cellular biophysics, slow developmental tuning, or all three? Manifold-constrained BCI learning (Sadtler 2014) makes the constraint concrete but leaves its substrate open. ([[vyas-2020-computation-through-dynamics|Vyas et al., 2020]])
- Are the motor-cortex CTD motifs (preparatory initial conditions, output-null preparation, rotational pattern generation, CIS) specific to motor cortex or instances of more general motifs? Rotations appear in speech cortex (Stavisky 2019) but not in SMA or muscles (Lara 2018a) — what predicts where the motif appears?
- Is null-space structure learned or given? Does the brain learn to place preparation in the null space of fixed readout weights `C`, or do `C` and cortical dynamics co-adapt?
- How widespread is the communication-subspace picture (Semedo 2019)? Does every inter-area connection factor cleanly into communication vs. private subspaces, analogous to output-null vs. output-potent in motor cortex?
- What is the biological implementation of contextual inputs in CTD models? Tonic thalamocortical input (Wang 2018) is one candidate; neuromodulation is another — are these distinct mechanisms or different labels for related signals?
- What is the relationship between fast within-repertoire learning (hours, Golub 2018) and slow off-manifold learning (days, Oby 2019)? Are these distinct mechanisms (synaptic tagging, consolidation) or a continuum? Does [[Memory Consolidation|SWR-driven consolidation]] mediate the slow, structural change?
- Can CTD be unified with credit-assignment accounts ([[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur et al., 2020]])? CTD describes what the dynamics *do*; credit-assignment describes how synapses get tuned to produce them — the two should compose but have not been worked out together.
- When brains and RNNs trained on the same task find the same computational scaffold via different strategies ([[brennan-2023-looper-computational-scaffold|Brennan 2023]]), what does this tell us about how to use RNNs as CTD hypothesis generators? Which RNN predictions should we take seriously as biological hypotheses and which should we treat as dataset-specific artifacts?
- Can the rotational motif in motor cortex be causally tested by perturbing the rotation directly (e.g., via patterned optogenetic input along the rotation axis)? The tools are becoming available; the critical falsification experiment for CTD as a causal model has not been done.

## Circuit-Behavior Mapping & State-Dependent Reconfiguration
*(New — added from Banerjee et al. 2023)*

- What other *C. elegans* sensorimotor circuits show life-stage-dependent reconfiguration? Is CO₂ chemotaxis an outlier, or is stage-dependent circuit identity the norm? ([[banerjee-2023-life-stage-chemosensation|Banerjee et al., 2023]])
- What is the molecular mechanism by which daf-2 insulin/IGF signaling gates AIB's recruitment into the dauer CO₂ circuit? Does it alter innexin composition (CHE-7 abundance or phosphorylation), receptor expression, or intrinsic excitability — or all three?
- Are there analogous state-dependent gap-junction reconfigurations in mammalian circuits? Electrical synapses are present throughout vertebrate nervous systems; whether they are similarly gated by systemic signals (insulin, cortisol, sex hormones) at the level of effective connectivity is largely unexplored.
- Does the same sensory neuron (BAG) carry different information in the two life stages, or does the downstream divergence transform a common signal differently? The reduced BAG response amplitude in dauers hints at the former, but calcium imaging resolution is insufficient to settle it.
- Is there a principled way to read off the effective connectome from a physiological state vector? Connectomics currently yields a static wiring diagram; a framework for computing the state-dependent active subset is missing.
- Does the [[Successor Representation]] / [[Endotaxis]] formal overlap generalize in the presence of circuit reconfiguration? If the same sensory gradient is routed through different interneurons in different states, does that correspond to different "value functions" implemented over the same anatomical substrate?
- How widespread is neuromodulation-via-electrical-synapse-recruitment as a mechanism? Classical neuromodulation (dopamine, serotonin, norepinephrine) operates on chemical synapse gain; Banerjee shows a systemic endocrine signal acting on electrical synapse composition. Is this a distinct class of neuromodulation or a widely distributed one that has been under-measured?
- Do analogous *intra-stage* state reconfigurations exist (feeding vs. sated, arousal state, circadian phase), or is the life-stage case special? Crustacean stomatogastric literature suggests the former; the Banerjee-level genetic precision has not been applied.

## Random Networks, Chaos & Edge of Chaos
*(New — added from Sompolinsky, Crisanti & Sommers 1988)*

- What changes when recurrent couplings are **structured** rather than i.i.d. Gaussian? Dale's law, sparsity, and low-rank task-relevant perturbations (Mastrogiuseppe & Ostojic 2018; Schuessler 2020) shift or restructure the chaotic transition — how generic is the "random background + low-rank task component" decomposition of biological connectivity, and is the brain well-described that way? ([[sompolinsky-1988-chaos-random-networks|SCS 1988]])
- Does cortex operate at the [[Edge of Chaos|edge of chaos]] (gJ ≈ 1), and if so, through what homeostatic mechanism is the operating point maintained? Candidate knobs include synaptic scaling, intrinsic excitability regulation, and inhibitory gain control — none has been causally tied to a criticality-preserving setpoint.
- What is the **manifold dimensionality** of the SCS chaotic flow, and how does it relate to the ~10D intrinsic [[Neural Manifolds|manifolds]] observed in motor cortex? Is there a principled mapping from connectivity statistics to the dimensionality of the resulting attractor?
- How does the chaos transition shift under **biological realism** — conductance-based neurons instead of rate, spiking variability, finite population sizes, short-range rather than fully-connected coupling? The SCS result is exact in the N → ∞ i.i.d. limit; its biological analog has not been worked out for any specific cortical microcircuit.
- Is cortical trial-to-trial variability primarily **deterministic chaos** from recurrent dynamics, **stochastic channel noise** at the single-neuron level, or a mixture? SCS shows deterministic chaos can generate apparent noise; balanced E-I networks suggest the same at the spiking level; a clean experimental dissociation remains elusive.
- What is the relationship between SCS-style chaos and the **limit-cycle attractors** observed in *C. elegans* ([[brennan-2019-conserved-macroscopic-dynamics|Brennan & Proekt, 2019]])? Is the worm's nervous system operating below gJ = 1 with structured couplings producing limit cycles, or is it in a distinct dynamical regime that the random-coupling ensemble doesn't capture?
- Does the DMFT formalism extend meaningfully to **networks with plasticity**, where connectivity is itself dynamic? Most DMFT work treats J as quenched disorder; a self-consistent theory of the joint activity-connectivity dynamics would close a conceptual gap between circuit dynamics and learning.

## Balanced E-I Networks & Cortical Operating Point
*(New — added from Brunel 2000)*

- Does cortex **actively maintain a specific operating point** in the (g, ν_ext) plane, and if so where? The [[brunel-1999-sparsely-connected-networks|Brunel 2000]] phase diagram gives four regimes (SR, AR, AI, SI) separated by Hopf and saddle-node bifurcations; the [[Asynchronous Irregular State|AI]] state matches spontaneous cortical activity, but whether the brain is poised near an AI/SI boundary (to allow gamma/ripple excursions) or well inside AI (for robustness) is an experimental question with implications for every downstream computation.
- What changes when **synaptic time constants** are realistic rather than pure delays? Brunel 2000 uses δ-function PSCs with a fixed transmission delay D; realistic synapses have finite rise/decay times (AMPA vs. NMDA vs. GABA_A vs. GABA_B) that should modify the positions of the Hopf bifurcations and the frequencies of gamma and ripple oscillations. Does the qualitative four-regime structure survive?
- Can **gamma and sharp-wave ripples** really be unified as Hopf bifurcations of a single balanced network, or are they products of genuinely distinct cell-type-specific generators? Brunel's model has a single undifferentiated inhibitory population; the biology has PV+ (fast, perisomatic, ≈ gamma generator) and gap-junction-coupled populations ( ≈ ripple generator), among others. A cell-type-resolved extension of the Brunel framework would test whether the two oscillations can still be collapsed onto the same phase diagram.
- How do **low-rank, task-relevant perturbations** (Mastrogiuseppe & Ostojic 2018; Schuessler 2020) overlay on a Brunel-style balanced AI background? The [[Computation Through Dynamics|CTD]] motifs — rotations, line attractors, preparatory initial conditions — that get carved out of the AI substrate by training should show up as low-rank departures from the random-connectivity background. What is the connection-count budget for task structure before the AI state itself destabilizes?
- Does the **Dale's law constraint** matter for the qualitative phase diagram? Brunel 2000 imposes Dale's law by construction (separate E and I populations with fixed signs); the SCS ensemble does not. Whether Dale's law shifts, preserves, or restructures the transition to chaos is a specific question about how biological connectivity constraints translate to dynamical regime.
- Can the Brunel framework be extended to **temporally correlated external input** rather than white noise? Real cortical input is slow (ν_ext from thalamus is itself rhythmic and autocorrelated). Colored input should sharpen some resonances and broaden others — a necessary step before comparing the model's oscillatory predictions to LFP and MUA data quantitatively.
- Is there a unified description that smoothly interpolates between **[[sompolinsky-1988-chaos-random-networks|SCS chaos]]** (rate, asymmetric Gaussian J, gain gJ) and **Brunel AI** (spiking, Dale's law, (g, ν_ext))? Conceptually they are the same phenomenon — random-network irregularity as an intrinsic property of the connectivity ensemble — but no single framework derives both transitions from a common starting point. This matters because it would tell us whether "AI" and "chaos" are two names for one thing or two distinct critical phenomena.

## Wilson-Cowan & Structured Rate-Level E-I Dynamics
*(New — added from Wilson & Cowan 1972)*

- Can the Wilson-Cowan **regime inequalities** be measured directly in cortex? The critical predictions are on population-level aggregates (c₁ = aggregate E-E coupling × cell density, etc.). Paired whole-cell recordings plus anatomical reconstructions could in principle estimate these aggregates; whether they sit above the limit-cycle threshold (`c₁aₑ > c₄aᵢ + 18`), above the hysteresis threshold (`c₁ > 9/aₑ`), or both, in specific cortical areas is an empirical question with direct regime-selection implications. ([[wilson-cowan-1972-ei-populations|Wilson & Cowan, 1972]])
- Is **Theorem 4** (limit-cycle-capable populations are also hysteresis-capable under different bias) experimentally verifiable in a single cortical population? The prediction: a population that oscillates (e.g., during gamma-band visual processing) should, under different tonic drive or neuromodulation, exhibit bistability (persistent activity). Does a single cortical area cross between these regimes on physiological timescales, and what signals drive it across?
- How do the WC regime boundaries **shift under realistic ingredients** — adaptation, short-term plasticity, dendritic compartments, conductance-based input? The 1972 equations use fixed refractoriness and sigmoid response; each added ingredient should restructure the boundaries. Which regimes survive, and which depend on the simplified form?
- Does the **Freeman-style damped oscillation** interpretation of evoked potentials survive modern large-scale recording? WC identifies the ~200 ms ringing after a cortical stimulus as the signature of a stable fixed point with complex-conjugate Jacobian eigenvalues. Systematic mapping of evoked-potential damping times across cortical areas, and whether areas that generate intrinsic limit cycles have shorter damping times (closer to the Hopf boundary), is testable.
- What is the **biological interpretation of the sigmoid slope parameters (aₑ, aᵢ)**? The WC regime inequalities depend on these as much as on the couplings. Slope maps to the distribution of thresholds in the population (sharper slope = tighter threshold distribution). Are inhibitory interneurons more threshold-homogeneous (steeper aᵢ) than pyramidal cells (shallower aₑ), and does the difference match what the inequalities predict for typical cortical operating regimes?
- Can a **single neural-mass description** capture the full gamma/ripple/theta/delta repertoire of cortex, or does each rhythm require its own structured network? WC produces one limit cycle per regime; Jansen-Rit adds a third variable (pyramidal subpopulation) and reproduces alpha; TVB chains many WC-like units across cortical areas. Whether there is a compact mesoscopic description that reproduces the full rhythm repertoire, or whether rhythms are irreducibly circuit-type-specific, is open.
- Does the **mesoscopic parameters → macroscopic regime** mapping give a quantitative form of the genes→control→structure hypothesis? The prediction: given a distribution over ion-channel expression and synapse counts (set by gene expression), the distribution over (c₁...c₄, aₑ, aᵢ) should be concentrated enough that the target regime emerges with high probability across individuals. Making this quantitative — estimating the gene→parameter map and the parameter→regime stability margin — would turn the conceptual picture into a testable probabilistic claim. ([[Degeneracy]])
- Is the **stochastic Wilson-Cowan / path-integral framework** (Buice & Cowan 2007, 2009) the right next step for bridging WC mean-field descriptions with critical phenomena (neuronal avalanches, power-law scaling)? The conceptual mapping — finite-N fluctuations around the WC attractor as a fluctuating field theory — is attractive, but whether it matches experimental avalanche statistics quantitatively across cortical areas is an active open question.

## Helmholtz Machine & Variational Inference in Cortex
*(New — added from Dayan, Hinton, Neal & Zemel 1994)*

- Does the brain implement anything resembling the **[[Wake-Sleep Algorithm|wake-sleep algorithm]]**? The most testable prediction is that cortical recognition pathways are fine-tuned by offline generative activity — e.g., REM-like sampling from an internal generative model should improve recognition of patterns similar to those hallucinated. Is there empirical evidence for a REM-specific tuning of recognition pathways, distinct from SWS consolidation? ([[dayan-hinton-1994-helmholtz-machine|Dayan et al., 1994]])
- Are the [[Credit Assignment|dendritic credit-assignment lineage]] and the [[Wake-Sleep Algorithm|wake-sleep]] lineage **formally equivalent** under some mapping, or do they occupy genuinely distinct algorithmic niches? Identifying what each can learn that the other cannot would sharpen the biological predictions of both.
- The Helmholtz machine uses a **purely feed-forward recognition pathway** for biological speed. But cortex has massive lateral and top-down connectivity, and real perception is iterative (figure-ground, ambiguous figures, attentional modulation). What is the right framework for **iterative amortized inference** in networks with local connectivity? [[dayan-hinton-1994-helmholtz-machine|Dayan et al.]] gesture at within-layer iteration (columnar microstructure with local excitation and longer-range inhibition) as an extension — has anyone made this concrete?
- How does the **factorial Q** assumption interact with the correlated posteriors observed in real cortical populations? Real posteriors over object identity, position, etc. are highly non-factorial; the factorial approximation throws these correlations away. Structured variational methods (mixture of factorials, normalizing flows) relax this at a cost — does the brain use something like them, or does it accept the approximation penalty?
- The wake-sleep algorithm follows an **incorrect gradient** in the sleep phase. Modern fixes (reweighted wake-sleep, stacked-RBM pretraining) exist in ML but have no clean biological analog. Does the brain implement a correction, or tolerate the bias?
- Does the Helmholtz architecture scale naturally to **continuous latent variables** (pose, position, speed, muscle-length targets) that are central to sensorimotor control? Modern VAEs do this via reparameterization, but the biological implementation would require analog (rate-coded) latents with analog prediction errors — bringing the framework closer to hierarchical predictive coding.
- How does the Helmholtz machine reconcile with [[Computation Through Dynamics|CTD]]? The recognition pathway is explicitly non-recurrent; CTD treats cortex as a recurrent dynamical system whose state *is* the computation. Real cortex is both; a framework that combines amortized inference with intrinsic recurrent dynamics is missing.
- Is there a principled way to tell whether cortex is running something closer to **Helmholtz-style amortized inference** (feed-forward + generative sampling for offline tuning) vs. **CTD-style recurrent dynamics** (state trajectories that *are* the computation)? These make different predictions about how activity should look during recognition, imagination, and sleep.

## Emergent Behavior & Autonomy
*(New — added from Li et al. 2024)*

- Can prediction + noise scale beyond toy environments to handle delayed rewards, high-dimensional sensory input, and aversive stimuli?
- What is the relationship between PaN's noise-driven exploration and norepinephrine/locus coeruleus modulation of neural gain?
- If reward-seeking is "free" (emerges from prediction + noise), what exactly are dopamine and explicit reward circuits *for*? Is their role primarily to bias which emergent goals get preferred (via learning rate modulation) rather than to define reward?
- How does PaN's mechanism relate to specific biological circuits? Is it relevant to any real neural system, or purely a proof of sufficiency?

## FORCE Learning & Trained Chaotic Networks
*(New — added from Sussillo & Abbott 2009)*

- What biologically plausible rule could approximate FORCE's **fast plasticity** requirement? RLS updates weights on millisecond timescales with aggressive per-PC effective rates — no known cortical plasticity mechanism operates this quickly. Candidates include short-term plasticity acting as a fast cache for slower long-term changes, neuromodulator-gated windows of elevated plasticity, or dendritic mechanisms that allow local fast computation without requiring fast synaptic turnover at the whole-axon level. ([[sussillo-abbott-2009-force-learning|Sussillo & Abbott, 2009]])
- How does the brain generate the **error signal** that FORCE-style learning requires? In architecture 1B (readout trained to drive a fixed generator), the error is defined at the readout. In architecture 1C (within-generator internal modifications), the same global scalar error must reach every internal synapse. For motor learning, candidates include cerebellar climbing fibers (Marr-Albus-Ito), basal-ganglia dopamine prediction errors, and proprioceptive mismatch signals — but the biological mapping from behavioral error to synapse-level teaching signal remains under-specified.
- Does the three-architecture taxonomy (1A readout-feedback, 1B separate-feedback-network, 1C internal-modifications) map onto a concrete **motor cortex + cerebellum/basal ganglia division of labor**? Architecture 1B suggests cerebellum or basal ganglia as a trainable feedback subsystem driving a relatively fixed cortical generator; architecture 1C suggests M1 internal plasticity as the substrate for virtuoso fine-motor acquisition. Direct experimental tests — e.g., lesioning or silencing the cerebellar/BG "feedback" during motor learning while recording M1 dynamics — would distinguish these hypotheses.
- Is the **operational edge of chaos** (largest `g` where FORCE still suppresses chaos, roughly 1.5, failure above 1.56) related to the theoretical edge (`gJ = 1` in SCS)? The gap between the two suggests that "maximally trainable" is not the same as "marginally chaotic" — training benefits from being *inside* the chaotic regime, not at its boundary. What is the principled relationship between trainability and Lyapunov exponent in recurrent networks?
- Does FORCE's **preparation-as-chaos-suppression** reading of Churchland 2006's variability drop generalize? A testable prediction: during preparation, cortical dynamics should look less chaotic (lower effective Lyapunov exponent, reduced trial-to-trial divergence) than during rest or spontaneous activity. Methods for estimating Lyapunov exponents from neural population recordings are maturing — applying them to PMd during the preparation period would directly test the framework.
- Can FORCE-style training **scale to closed-loop sensorimotor control** where the environment is part of the feedback loop? The 2009 paper trains on open-loop target functions; real motor learning involves the body and world as part of the dynamical system. Whether the RLS rule works when the "readout" drives an actuator whose sensory consequences loop back as inputs, with realistic transmission delays, has been explored in follow-up work but remains an open direction — particularly whether training stability survives the additional feedback delay.
- How does FORCE's **global scalar error + recurrent dynamics** credit assignment compose with the [[Credit Assignment|dendritic lineage]] (spatial partitioning + slower plasticity)? These are different trades in the same design space; cortex plausibly uses both. A natural hypothesis: dendritic credit assignment handles cross-layer structural learning on slow timescales, while FORCE-style global-error mechanisms (driven by cerebellum or BG) handle fast within-region fine-tuning of trained dynamics. Is there direct evidence for this division?
- What determines the `~8` output-relevant and `~50` uniquely-determined principal components in FORCE-trained networks? Is the `~8` set by the complexity of the trained output, the `~50` by some structural property of the chaotic reservoir, or both? The empirical ~10D motor-cortex manifold matches the ~8 figure — is this coincidence, or does it reflect a shared scaling law?

## Spin-Glass Theory & Associative-Memory Capacity
*(New — added from Parisi 1979)*

- Does cortical associative memory exhibit any **ultrametric organization** of its stored patterns? Parisi's hierarchical RSB predicts that pure states in disordered systems with frustration cluster ultrametrically — pairwise overlaps between any three pure states satisfy `q(a,c) ≥ min(q(a,b), q(b,c))`. The empirical signature would be: among many recalled memories, the distribution of pairwise overlaps shows a hierarchical clustering structure (tree-like) rather than uniform or modular. Tools from neural-population analysis could in principle test this on hippocampal or PFC ensembles. ([[parisi-1979-replica-symmetry-breaking|Parisi, 1979]])
- Is **replica symmetry broken in trained recurrent networks**? Trained RNNs (under [[Computation Through Dynamics|CTD]]) carve structured low-dimensional manifolds out of a chaotic random-network reservoir. The static analog of this question — whether the equilibrium overlap distribution between trained-fixed-point patterns shows RSB-like hierarchical structure — connects directly to whether CTD-style dynamics inherit any of the spin-glass capacity bounds. Largely unexplored.
- Where does **RSB intersect the [[Edge of Chaos]] picture**? The de Almeida–Thouless line (where the replica-symmetric solution becomes locally unstable) is a static analog of the SCS chaos transition. Both are points where simple Ansätze fail and richer structure becomes necessary. Are there biological circuits whose effective parameters sit near both transitions, and what would the joint operating-point look like?
- Does the **sparse-coding capacity boost** for [[attractor-dynamics|Hopfield-style memory]] match the empirical sparseness of cortical and hippocampal codes? Sparse codes raise the dense-Hopfield bound `α_c ≈ 0.138` substantially, sometimes to capacity scaling super-linearly in `N`. Quantitative comparison between the predicted sparse-Hopfield capacity and the observed pattern-density / capacity in real associative-memory regions (CA3, mushroom body) would be a direct test of the theory's biological reach.
- How does the **modern (dense) Hopfield network** capacity (Krotov-Hopfield 2016, Ramsauer 2020) — which achieves exponential storage by using a softmax-like energy that avoids the spin-glass regime entirely — relate to the original Parisi/AGS bound? The structural analog of the Parisi solution in this regime is not fully worked out, and connects directly to the energy-based reading of transformer attention.
- Does **spin-glass theory predict capacity bounds for [[force-learning|FORCE-trained]] RNNs**? The static-replica machinery would, in principle, bound the number of distinct trajectories that can be stably encoded by a fixed-size chaotic reservoir under FORCE-style training. The corresponding calculation has not, to our knowledge, been done — and would tell us whether the ~8/~50-PC structure that emerges in trained chaotic RNNs is theoretically necessary or merely empirical.
- **Aging and slow relaxation** in spin glasses are striking phenomena (autocorrelations that never reach equilibrium, response that depends on quench history). Does anything analogous happen in biological associative memory? Cortex operates on fast timescales, so direct aging is unlikely — but slow consolidation phenomena (memory traces that strengthen or reorganize over weeks/months even without rehearsal) might be a distant cousin of the same dynamics.

## Hopfield Networks & Attractor-Memory Biology
*(New — added from Hopfield 1982)*

- What is the right **asymmetric extension** of the Hopfield network for sequence memory? [[hopfield-1982-collective-computational-abilities|Hopfield's (1982)]] sequence experiment (asymmetric `ΔT_ij = A Σ (2V^{s+1} − 1)(2V^s − 1)`) gets sequences of length ~4 before breaking. Asymmetric Hopfield variants (Sompolinsky-Kanter 1986; Buhmann-Schulten 1987) extend this; modern training-based approaches ([[sussillo-abbott-2009-force-learning|FORCE]]) bypass it entirely by training arbitrary trajectories into chaotic networks. What is the **theoretical limit** on how many distinct sequences a fixed-size recurrent network can store and reliably traverse, and how does it compare to the static `α_c ≈ 0.138 N` bound?
- **Catastrophic interference vs. graceful forgetting in biology.** Hopfield's saturation-bounded `T_ij` gives natural forgetting of distant memories without requiring an explicit "forgetting circuit." How does this compare to biological forgetting, which is selective, content-dependent, and modulated by sleep / consolidation? Is bounded synaptic strength a sufficient mechanism, or does the brain implement explicit forgetting rules? The connection to [[memory-consolidation|systems consolidation]] and SWR-driven replay is unexplored.
- **Does cortex ever cross the spin-glass-phase transition?** AGS (1985, 1987) show that above `α_c ≈ 0.138 N`, stored Hopfield patterns are replaced by spurious spin-glass minima with no relation to assigned memories. If biological associative-memory regions (CA3, mushroom body) ever cross this transition under pathological overload, the failure mode would be **retrieval of confabulated memories** rather than failure to retrieve. Is there empirical evidence for this kind of failure mode — e.g., in dementia, schizophrenia, or experimentally induced overload?
- **Familiarity recognition by initial processing rate.** Hopfield's striking observation that, at extreme overload (`n = 500, N = 100`), familiar vs. unfamiliar inputs can still be discriminated by **initial processing rate** rather than activity content predicts a rate-coded familiarity signal in real associative regions — faster dynamics in familiar cortical states than unfamiliar. Is there a corresponding biological signal? The question connects to the perirhinal cortex / "familiarity-without-recollection" literature.
- **Dale's law and the Hopfield framework.** Real cortical neurons obey Dale's law (a neuron's outgoing synapses are uniformly excitatory or uniformly inhibitory), but the standard Hebbian rule freely produces both signs of `T_ij` for the same pre-synaptic neuron across post-synaptic targets. Dale-constrained Hopfield variants (Amit-Brunel 1997; Brunel 2003) preserve attractor structure with separate E and I populations, but the **capacity** under Dale's-law constraints (relative to dense unconstrained `0.138 N`) remains less cleanly characterized. Does the cortex's Dale's law cost capacity or organize it?
- **Hopfield 1982's "small groups of cells" remark and dendritic credit assignment.** Hopfield notes that "the Hebbian property need not reside in single synapses; small groups of cells which produce such a net effect would suffice." The [[credit-assignment|dendritic credit-assignment lineage]] (Kording → Sacramento → Payeur → Greedy → [[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni]]) makes this concrete: the Hebbian unit is a coordinated pyramidal-cell + interneuron + dendritic-compartment microcircuit, not a single synapse. Can a Hopfield-like associative-memory model with a *coordinated multi-cell unit* as its plasticity primitive be analytically characterized, and does it inherit the same `0.138 N` bound?
- **Pattern completion in CA3 vs. modern Hopfield.** Hippocampal CA3 is the textbook biological substrate for a Hopfield-style associative memory, but the modern (dense) Hopfield network (Krotov-Hopfield 2016; Ramsauer 2020) achieves **exponential** capacity with a softmax-like energy and has been shown equivalent to transformer attention. Is CA3 closer to the dense-quadratic Hopfield (with `0.138 N` capacity) or to a modern-Hopfield variant (with much larger effective capacity)? The empirical sparseness of CA3 codes pushes one way; the limited ~10⁵-pattern rough estimate of CA3 capacity pushes another. The question connects to whether the brain's associative memory is fundamentally quadratic or super-quadratic in its energy structure.

## Hierarchical Predictive Coding & Cortical Inference
*(New — added from Rao & Ballard 1999)*

- **Are layer 2/3 pyramidal neurons specifically the prediction-error carriers in cortex?** [[rao-ballard-1999-predictive-coding|Rao & Ballard (1999)]] predict that layer 2/3 (the source of feedforward axons to higher areas) carries the residual `(r − r^td)`. Mouse V1 work (Keller-Mrsic-Flogel 2018; Attinger 2017; Zmarz & Keller 2016) provides initial evidence for prediction-error signals in superficial pyramidal cells, but the full layer-resolved picture — which cell types in which layers carry predictions vs. errors vs. estimates — is incompletely characterized. The [[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento microcircuit]] supplies one detailed proposal at the dendritic level.
- **Do all extra-classical RF effects fail when feedback is silenced, or only some?** The cat-cooling, monkey-cryoloop, and recent optogenetic feedback-silencing results give partial confirmation, but a clean test across stimulus types (does pop-out survive V2 silencing? what about texture-context modulation? what about MT direction-of-motion suppression with MST silenced?) remains incomplete.
- **Does the hierarchical predictive coding framework scale to V2, V4, IT?** The Fig. 2c level-2 receptive fields in [[rao-ballard-1999-predictive-coding|Rao-Ballard]] look qualitatively like combinations of level-1 oriented features, but real V2 has shape-sensitive and illusory-contour neurons that the model doesn't obviously reproduce. Whether scaling Rao-Ballard up captures higher visual cortex (and what additional ingredients — nonlinearities, sparsity, lateral interactions — are required) is open.
- **What is the biological substrate of the precision weights `(1/σ², 1/σ²_td)`?** Friston's later work argues precision is set by attention, neuromodulation (acetylcholine, dopamine), and population-level synchronization. The concrete biological mapping from "precision" to a measurable cortical signal is the most actively debated open question in the predictive-coding literature.
- **Is V1 actually running predictive coding, or are sparse coding / ICA / efficient coding empirically indistinguishable?** Many of the same Gabor-like RFs emerge from sparse coding ([[sparse-coding|Olshausen-Field 1996]]), ICA (Bell-Sejnowski 1997), and other efficient-coding principles. Distinguishing predictive coding from these alternatives empirically requires going beyond static-RF predictions to dynamics, feedback dependence, and the specific extra-classical effects the framework predicts. The feedback-inactivation prediction is the cleanest test — but the alternatives have their own predictions about feedback that have not been fully worked out.
- **How does the framework reconcile with [[computation-through-dynamics|CTD]]?** Rao-Ballard treats sensory cortex as an equilibrium-finding inference machine; CTD treats motor and decision cortices as dynamical systems whose temporal evolution *is* the computation. Are these two genuinely distinct cortical operating modes (sensory vs. motor; equilibrium vs. trajectory), or two limits of a unified framework? Higher cognitive areas like [[Prefrontal Cortex|PFC]] sit ambiguously between the two — a unified treatment is undeveloped.
- **Does temporal predictive coding (predicting future inputs from past) have the same architectural signature as spatial predictive coding (predicting center from surround)?** [[rao-ballard-1999-predictive-coding|Rao-Ballard]] briefly mention extension to temporal prediction via recurrent weights `V` (Methods). The retina/LGN literature (Srinivasan-Laughlin-Dubs 1982; Dong-Atick 1995) shows clean temporal predictive coding at the early visual stages. Whether the cortical hierarchy implements *spatial-temporal* predictive coding with a unified architecture, or whether different mechanisms handle the two axes, is open. The connection to [[computation-through-dynamics|CTD]] preparatory dynamics is suggestive but unworked-out.
- **How does the perceptual Rao-Ballard machinery interface with the [[active-inference|active-inference]] action loop?** Rao-Ballard is purely perceptual; Friston's extension makes the framework a control theory by adding action. The biological circuit that *implements* the action-augmented free-energy minimization — connecting prediction-error signals from sensory cortex to motor commands — is not a worked-out part of the Rao-Ballard model. The bridge to [[motor-cortex|motor cortex]] CTD dynamics, basal-ganglia value computation, and the [[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|PaN-style]] noise-driven reward-seeking is the open frontier of the framework.
- **Does the [[dark-room-problem|dark-room problem]] really not arise for pure perceptual Rao-Ballard?** The Rao-Ballard generative model implicitly contains natural-image priors that prevent dark-room solutions for perception (the cortex is trained to predict natural scenes, so a dark room would be highly unpredictable). But adding action makes the dark-room objection live: an agent that minimizes prediction error could in principle act to occupy maximally predictable environments. The standard responses (evolved priors over preferred states, exploration drives, expected-free-energy formulations) all add structure beyond the Rao-Ballard objective; whether any of them is biologically realized is open.

## MaxEnt, Bayesian Brain & the Constraint-Discovery Problem
*(New — added from Jaynes 1957)*

- **The constraint-discovery problem: which sufficient statistics should the brain register?** [[jaynes-1957-maxent-statistical-mechanics|Jaynes (1957)]] / [[maximum-entropy-principle|MaxEnt]] determines the prior given the constraints, but says nothing about *which* observables (sufficient statistics) an organism should choose to track over evolutionary and developmental time. This is the deep gap between formal Bayesian inference and the brain's actual problem of building useful world-models. Is there a principled second-order theory — MaxEnt over constraint-sets, or some analogous criterion — that picks which constraints to register? The question is the formal version of "what do [[sparse-coding|sparse codes]] encode, and why those features?" and connects directly to the slow timescale of representation building observed in [[reinert-2021-pfc-categorization|Reinert et al. (2021)]] and articulated in [[meister-2022-learning-fast-slow|Meister (2022)]].
- **Where in the cortex are MaxEnt priors physically encoded?** If predictive-coding circuits are computing MaxEnt-like inferences ([[rao-ballard-1999-predictive-coding|Rao & Ballard 1999]]), *which* anatomical structures encode the constraints? Synaptic weight distributions? Recurrent dynamics? Dendritic compartments? Population-activity statistics? The Jaynes framework is implementation-agnostic, but biology is not. Pinning constraints to specific cellular substrates would let one predict how priors should change under specific perturbations (LTP/LTD, dendritic plasticity, NDNF+ interneuron silencing à la [[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni 2026]]).
- **Non-equilibrium MaxEnt for neural dynamics.** [[jaynes-1957-maxent-statistical-mechanics|Jaynes (1957)]] is explicit that his framework is for *equilibrium* prediction; time-dependent phenomena require additional structure. Real brains are out-of-equilibrium dynamical systems on every timescale shorter than learning. The non-equilibrium extensions (max-caliber / path-MaxEnt) exist in physics but their application to neural dynamics is largely open. What is the right inference-theoretic framework for the temporal evolution of cortical activity, and does it reduce to anything like the equilibrium MaxEnt prior on familiar timescales?
- **Reading out registered constraints from neural population activity.** Maximum-entropy models of multi-neuron spike trains (Schneidman, Berry, Segev & Bialek 2006; Tkačik et al. 2014) fit a MaxEnt distribution to recorded activity using only pairwise correlations and ask how well it predicts higher-order statistics. This is the most direct empirical instance of MaxEnt-brain inference in the wiki — but the theoretical question of *why* pairwise constraints should be the right cut for retina (and whether higher cortical areas need richer constraint sets) is unresolved. Can we read out, from a recorded neural population, which "MaxEnt constraints" it has come to use over learning?
- **Does the dark-room objection dissolve under the MaxEnt reading?** The standard [[dark-room-problem|dark-room]] / [[active-inference|active-inference]] worry is that free-energy-minimizing agents should seek maximally predictable environments. Under the MaxEnt lens this looks different: an agent that *registers richer constraints* over time (i.e., extends control over more latent variables) has a *higher*-entropy prior, not a lower one. The dark-room agent has impoverished its constraint set, which is the opposite of what MaxEnt-style intelligence does. Is this re-framing actually different from existing responses (preferred priors, expected free energy, intrinsic motivation), or is it the same content in different language?
- **Generalized "temperatures" for non-energy quantities.** Jaynes generalizes Clausius to all conjugate-pair constraints: each registered constraint `f_k` has its own conjugate Lagrange multiplier `λ_k` that plays the role of an inverse "temperature." If the brain runs MaxEnt-style inference on multiple sufficient statistics, are there empirically meaningful "temperatures" for non-energy quantities — e.g., a "synaptic-strength temperature" set by neuromodulation, a "spatial-frequency temperature" set by attention? The framework predicts they exist; whether any of them have been measured in the neuromodulation / attention literature without being recognized as such is an interesting question.
- **Does the relationship between sparse coding and MaxEnt under kurtosis constraints sharpen the empirical predictions of [[sparse-coding|sparse coding]]?** If the heavy-tailed activity priors used in Olshausen-Field, ICA, and [[rao-ballard-1999-predictive-coding|Rao-Ballard]] are MaxEnt priors under specific moment constraints, the choice of constraint should make distinguishable empirical predictions for the moment structure of natural neural activity. Are kurtosis-constrained MaxEnt models testably different from log-prior-on-activity sparse-coding models in measurable ways?

## Free Energy Principle & Active Inference
*(New — added from Friston 2010)*

- **Is the [[free-energy-principle|free-energy principle]] empirically distinguishable from its rivals, or is it a unifying language?** Each of the nine "global" theories that [[friston-2010-free-energy-principle|Friston (2010)]] places under FEP — Bayesian brain, hierarchical [[predictive-coding|predictive coding]], [[efficient-coding-hypothesis|efficient/infomax]] coding, cell-assembly / correlation theory, biased competition, Neural Darwinism, optimal control / RL, game theory, dynamical-systems / attractor accounts — corresponds to FEP under stated assumptions, but each was developed without FEP and continues to be useful without it. Is FEP the *actual* unifying principle of cortex, or a unifying *language* for re-describing existing theories without making predictions distinguishable from them? The empirical bite of FEP, beyond the existing predictions of its constituent theories, is the key open question. Critics (Colombo & Wright 2018) have argued FEP is unfalsifiable as currently stated.
- **Does cortex actually adjust synaptic gain on prediction-error units in proportion to inverse error variance?** This is the cleanest concrete neural prediction in [[friston-2010-free-energy-principle|Friston (2010)]]: precision-weighting = synaptic gain modulation = attention. Mouse V1 work (Keller-Mrsic-Flogel 2018; Attinger 2017; Zmarz-Keller 2016) has begun to identify prediction-error-like signals in superficial pyramidal cells, but the precision-as-gain claim has not been directly tested. What experimental design would make the FEP-specific prediction distinguishable from the gain-modulation accounts that predate FEP (Reynolds-Chelazzi-Desimone 1999; Reynolds-Heeger 2009)?
- **Where do the priors come from?** [[friston-2010-free-energy-principle|FEP]] says agents minimize free energy under heritable phenotype-specific priors but does not specify the prior-discovery process. This is the embodied analog of the [[jaynes-1957-maxent-statistical-mechanics|Jaynes 1957]] constraint-discovery problem and is the place where FEP is most often accused of vacuity. The wiki's control-theoretic reading of [[Degeneracy]] (genes shape feedback control algorithms; conserved limit-cycle attractors emerge despite microscopic configuration) is a candidate evolutionary mechanism — but FEP itself is silent on it. Is the right answer Neural Darwinism, the genes→control→structure hypothesis, neither, or both at different timescales?
- **How does FEP relate to [[computation-through-dynamics|Computation Through Dynamics]]?** CTD-style trained recurrent dynamics — fixed points, line attractors, [[rotational-dynamics|rotational dynamics]], preparatory initial conditions — could in principle be derived as FEP minimizers under priors on hidden-state trajectories. But CTD is largely silent on generative models, and FEP is largely silent on the specific dynamical motifs CTD catalogues. Is there a real reconciliation (CTD-style trained dynamics as the time-extended fingerprint of FEP minimization), or do the two operate at different conceptual layers (FEP for sensory inference, CTD for motor and decision dynamics)?
- **Does FEP scale with the constraint-discovery problem when generative models must be structured / compositional?** Real organisms have priors over objects, faces, conspecifics, danger — structured, compositional priors that go beyond the Gaussian-noise hierarchical generative models that FEP-style implementations typically use. How much of this structure is genetically specified vs. developmentally constructed? FEP and Neural Darwinism both invoke selection at different timescales; the integration between selection at evolutionary time, developmental construction of the cortical generative model, and online inference / action under FEP is unresolved.
- **Is the destabilizing-priors approach to exploration (Friston 2010 mountain-car) a generalizable mechanism or an engineering trick?** [[friston-2010-free-energy-principle|Friston (2010)]] Figure 3 demonstrates that priors with negative friction in the cost function force the agent out of its starting basin and toward the goal. Does this scale to real-world animals with rich sensory niches and many goal states, or does the destabilizing-prior trick collapse into the same kind of ad-hoc engineering that FEP was meant to dispense with? Compare to [[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|PaN's]] noise-induced instability of the dark room as an alternative.
- **Are the FEP and PaN routes to closed-loop reward-seeking compatible, or genuinely alternative?** [[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|Li et al. (2024) PaN]] explicitly contrasts itself with active inference: closed-loop reward-seeking from prediction + noise without priors, recognition density, or modular planner. FEP says priors set the attractor landscape; PaN says noise prevents collapse to trivial fixed points within it. The two could be complementary (priors + noise) or alternative (priors vs. noise). Which is right empirically? Can a single experiment distinguish them in a real animal?
- **What is the relationship between active inference's modular implementations and biological emergence?** Most published active-inference models require separate modules for prior specification, recognition, and action selection. The wiki's organizing thesis prefers emergence from simple rules. Is FEP committed to modular implementation (in which case it is in tension with the thesis), or is the modularity a feature of current implementations rather than the principle itself? A non-modular biological implementation of FEP that emerges from local rules — analogous to how [[li-2024-prediction-noise-reward|PaN]] handles closed-loop control — would resolve the tension but does not yet exist.
- **Does the canonical-microcircuit reading (forward errors, backward predictions, precision = gain) compose with the [[credit-assignment|dendritic credit-assignment]] lineage?** [[friston-2010-free-energy-principle|Friston (2010)]]'s message-passing scheme has superficial pyramidal cells carrying prediction errors and deep pyramidal cells carrying predictions. The dendritic lineage ([[sacramento-2018-dendritic-microcircuits-backprop|Sacramento 2018]] etc.) splits prediction and prediction-error across apical vs basal compartments within single neurons. Are these two architectural readings of the same computation, or genuinely different proposals? Sacramento's prediction-error-as-credit-signal claim is consistent with FEP's reading, but the specific cell-level partitioning differs.

## Cellular Cognition & Molecular Memory
*(New — added from Kukushkin 2024)*

- **Which classical learning phenomena are reproducible in non-neural cells?** [[kukushkin-2024-massed-spaced-non-neural|Kukushkin et al. (2024)]] demonstrates the [[spacing-effect|spacing effect]]. The framework predicts other classical learning phenomena should be similarly reproducible: habituation (decreasing response to repeated stimuli), sensitization (heightened response after a strong stimulus), and possibly Pavlovian-style associative pairing if a way to deliver "CS" and "US" to the same cell with appropriate temporal coupling can be devised. Each is a distinct empirical question. The systematic mapping of which behavioral memory phenomena have cellular substrates and which are genuinely circuit-level is the open program the [[cellular-cognition|cellular-cognition]] framework opens.
- **Is the cellular spacing effect *causally* relevant for animal memory, or just correlated?** Kukushkin's case is mechanistic and elegant — same cascades, same dynamics in cells and in *Aplysia* sensory neurons — but not directly causal. A genetic / pharmacological intervention that alters the optimal ITI in cells *and* in animals, with the shift consistent with the cellular kinetic prediction, would close the loop. The Pagani et al. (2009) SHP2 phosphatase manipulation in *Drosophila* is the closest existing example, but lacks the cellular comparison.
- **Which kinetic parameters of the cascade set the 10-min optimal ITI, and how is this evolutionarily set?** The optimal ITI is conserved across species and cell types (5–20 min). Is this conservation because the kinetic time constants of PKA / PKC / ERK / CREB are physically constrained by the chemistry of the molecules involved (and so cannot be tuned independently in each species), or because evolution has converged on the same value despite the freedom to vary it? The first would make the spacing effect a chemistry-imposed feature of memory rather than an evolved adaptation; the second would make it a globally optimal solution. Selectively perturbing cascade kinetics in cells is the natural test.
- **Why does suboptimal training *down-regulate* total CREB?** [[kukushkin-2024-massed-spaced-non-neural|Kukushkin (2024)]] observed that 1×TPA and massed TPA reduce total CREB protein at 24 h. This active down-regulation suggests a homeostatic / interference protection mechanism that suppresses memory machinery in response to inadequate training. Is this a mechanism that prevents accumulation of weakly-encoded transcription? Does it have a behavioral analog (e.g., proactive interference, or the "less is more" effect in spaced practice)?
- **What is the role of the CREB-ERK positive feedback loop?** [[kukushkin-2024-massed-spaced-non-neural|Kukushkin (2024)]] observes that 666-15 (CREB inhibitor) reduces P-ERK at 24 h post-training, consistent with a CREB → BDNF → ERK feedback loop. The kinetics and gain of this loop should set the persistence of the consolidated state and may determine the boundary between "spaced training produces long-term memory" and "spaced training produces only short-term memory." A quantitative model that maps loop parameters to memory persistence does not yet exist.
- **Can CRE-luc cells be used to design optimal training protocols that predict memory outcomes in animals?** Zhang et al. (2011) computationally designed enhanced learning protocols using *Aplysia* cascade models. Kukushkin's CRE-luc system enables high-throughput empirical optimization. The pipeline (cell-derived training protocol → animal experiment) has not yet been closed. If it can be — predicted training schedules transfer from cells to mice or humans — the framework becomes practically useful for cognitive-enhancement and cognitive-disability applications.
- **What is the right formal framework for relating cellular memory to circuit memory?** Synapses add pairwise specificity; cellular cascades supply temporal-pattern decoding. But the formal model — what computations are uniquely circuit-level vs. uniquely cellular vs. shared — is undeveloped. A clean factorization would identify which memory phenomena require synapses (associative recall, pattern completion, sparse-code-dependent fast learning) and which do not (forgetting curves, optimal ITI, transcriptional consolidation). The [[cellular-cognition|cellular-cognition]] page tracks this.
- **Does cellular cognition modify the synapse-uniqueness claim in the [[synapses|synapses]] page?** The page previously asserted "no known non-neural system has true pairwise activity-dependent plasticity" with Physarum slime mold as the closest candidate. Kukushkin (2024) is consistent with this — non-neural cells do not show pairwise plasticity — but extends what *aspects* of memory can occur without synapses. The pairwise specificity claim is genuinely unique to synapses, but the temporal-pattern-decoding and transcriptional consolidation are not.

## What Does It Mean to Understand a Neural System?
*(New — added from Jonas & Kording 2017)*

- **Which neuroscience analytical methods recover [[marrs-levels-of-analysis|Marr-level]] understanding when validated against ground-truth-known artificial systems?** [[jonas-2017-microprocessor-critique|Jonas & Kording (2017)]] showed that connectomics, lesion studies, tuning curves, spike-word correlations, LFP / 1/f spectra, Granger causality, and NMF all fall short on a fully understood microprocessor. The constructive question is: which methods, possibly not yet in standard use, *do* recover the chip's algorithmic and computational hierarchy? Methods that explicitly search for hierarchical / modular structure, methods that demand counterfactual prediction (in the [[brennan-2023-looper-computational-scaffold|LOOPER]] mode), and methods that require trained-RNN sufficiency proofs are candidates, but their performance on the chip has not been systematically benchmarked.
- **Is a microprocessor the right test bed, or does it differ from biology in ways that make the analogy misleading?** The chip is deterministic, has only one transistor type, has clean modular boundaries, and was engineered top-down; brains are stochastic, have hundreds of neuron types, have fuzzy modular boundaries, and were evolved bottom-up. Some standard neuroscience methods may fail on the chip *because* of these differences (and would work on brains), while others may fail *despite* the chip being simpler (and would fail more severely on brains). Which side of this divide each method falls on is not established. Trained ANNs (where ground truth on representations is partial) and small biological systems (*C. elegans*, retina) are intermediate test beds.
- **Are sufficiency proofs strong enough to discharge the chip-NMF caveat for [[computation-through-dynamics|CTD]]?** A trained RNN that, run forward, generates the target behavior is constructive evidence for one possible mechanism — but the [[brennan-2023-looper-computational-scaffold|monkey-vs-RNN scaffold-but-different-strategy]] result shows that an RNN can match macroscopic dynamics without using the same algorithm. So sufficiency is necessary but not sufficient. What additional tests — counterfactual prediction, transferability across instances, generalization to novel stimuli — pin down the algorithm beyond the dynamics?
- **What does Lazebnik's "fix it" test look like in practice for cortical regions?** Memory neuroprosthetics (Berger et al. 2011), sensory neuroprosthetics, and motor BCIs gesture at one operationalization — replacing a region with a synthetic substitute. Where do these succeed in the strong sense ("the synthetic produces the same downstream computation as the biological tissue") versus the weak sense ("the synthetic produces *some* useful behavior")? A systematic accounting of partial successes and the levels at which they succeed is missing.
- **Can the [[degeneracy|degeneracy]] thread and the chip-style methodological mirror be unified?** Biological degeneracy: many implementations → one function. Chip methodological mirror: one implementation → many apparent functional types under standard analyses. Both are statements that the structure → function map is not invertible by current methods. Is there a single mathematical framework — perhaps grounded in the geometry of the underlying computation, or in the rank/dimensionality of the equivalence classes — that captures both phenomena? The [[circuit-behavior-mapping|circuit-behavior mapping]] page is the wiki's current container for the umbrella claim, but a formal account would be stronger.
- **What is the relationship between the constraint-discovery problem flagged for MaxEnt / FEP and the methods-validation problem flagged here?** [[jaynes-1957-maxent-statistical-mechanics|Jaynes]] and [[friston-2010-free-energy-principle|Friston]] both identify "which sufficient statistics / which priors should the brain use?" as the deep question their frameworks pose but do not solve. Jonas & Kording flag a parallel methodological version: "which features of neural data carry computational signal vs. epiphenomenon?" Both are constraint-selection problems at different levels. Whether progress on one informs the other (perhaps via shared evolutionary / ecological arguments) is an open thread.

## Coding, Subjective Physics, and the Status of Representational Claims
*(New — added from Brette 2018)*

- **What is the right representational primitive for directed relational structure?** [[binding-problem|Synchrony receptive fields]] (Brette 2012) handle symmetric relations like equality of sensory laws (`S_L(t) = S_R(t − d)` in the Jeffress 1948 ITD model), but synchrony is a symmetric relation and cannot directly carry directed content like "Paul drives car" vs. "car drives Paul." Neural syntax (Buzsáki 2010) — where the temporal sequence of activation encodes argument structure — is conceptually clean but lacks specific empirical support. Tensor-product representations (Smolensky 1990) are computationally adequate but lack neural plausibility. What carries directed structured relations in cortex remains the deepest open question for [[subjective-physics|subjective physics]] as a complete framework.
- **Can the dendritic credit-assignment lineage be re-read in [[subjective-physics|subjective-physics]] terms without losing its predictions?** The lineage from [[kording-konig-2001-two-integration-sites|Kording & Konig (2001)]] through [[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni et al. (2026)]] is internally framed as a credit-assignment mechanism for hierarchical predictive coding — apical-compartment signals are interpreted as prediction errors over latent causes. A subjective-physics reading would interpret the same biology as routing of *residual sensorimotor mismatch* (predicted vs. observed consequences of action) for local plasticity. The mechanism survives reinterpretation but the framing changes substantially. Whether the two readings make distinguishable empirical predictions — and which kind of teaching-signal-driven plasticity is actually observed in vivo — is open.
- **Are generative models compatible with subjective physics, or genuinely alternative?** [[brette-2018-coding-metaphor|Brette (2018)]] argues generative models map causes → observables and so inherit the symbol grounding problem; subjective physics specifies relations among observables and actions instead. But generative models are extraordinarily useful machinery in machine learning. Is there a sensorimotor-friendly reformulation in which generative models predict *consequences of actions* rather than *causes of inputs*? The distinction is subtle but consequential: in the first case the generative model is part of the controller; in the second it is a separable perceptual encoder. Most current implementations of [[active-inference|active inference]] are the second; whether the first is implementable and what it would predict differently are open questions.
- **What does a "model that behaves" look like at cortical scale?** Brette's prescription is principled but not algorithmic. The [[zhang-2024-endotaxis-neuromorphic-navigation|endotaxis circuit]] is one example of a sensorimotor-loop model that includes the controller (chemotaxis), the internal signal generator (map cells, goal cells), and the action module — at the scale of insect mushroom-body and proposed hippocampal navigation. What does the analogous specification look like for vision? Audition? Decision-making? The literature has plenty of "encoder" submodels but few full closed loops at cortical scale. The motor-cortex [[computation-through-dynamics|CTD]] literature is the closest existing approach, but its task-variable labeling is the residual coding-flavored move that subjective physics would push back on.
- **Does the wiki's coding-flavored language (place cells "encode" location, V1 "encodes" orientation, PFC "encodes" rules) survive Brette's critique?** The wiki's stance, following Brette, is that these claims should be read as *correspondence claims with experimental scope* — under the conditions of the experiment, neural activity statistically corresponds to the named variable — without inheriting the representational and causal entailments. Whether this hedge is consistently maintainable, or whether the language of the literature inevitably re-imports coding-style assumptions, is a methodological / conceptual open question for the wiki's curation.
- **What is the relation between [[subjective-physics|subjective physics]] and [[computation-through-dynamics|CTD]]?** CTD treats neural populations as dynamical systems whose evolution *is* the computation; subjective physics specifies what *kind* of relational structure that computation is over. The two are largely compatible — CTD is silent on what the dynamics represent, and subjective physics is silent on the dynamical mechanisms. Whether the integration is cleanly compositional (CTD = mechanism, subjective physics = content) or whether there are residual tensions (e.g., CTD's task-variable labeling vs. subjective physics' rejection of representations) is an open conceptual question.
- **Does the constraint-discovery problem flagged for [[maximum-entropy-principle|MaxEnt]] / [[free-energy-principle|FEP]] also apply to subjective physics?** [[jaynes-1957-maxent-statistical-mechanics|Jaynes]] flagged "which sufficient statistics should the brain register?" as the deep open question for MaxEnt. [[friston-2010-free-energy-principle|Friston]] flagged the analogous question for FEP — where do the heritable phenotype-specific priors come from? Subjective physics has its own version: which sensorimotor laws does the organism build models of? Why these and not others? Brette is largely silent on this, but the question maps onto the wiki's existing thread on the role of evolution and developmental construction in shaping neural priors. The dendritic credit-assignment lineage's "biased developmental construction of feedback alignments" ([[guerguiev-2017-segregated-dendrites|Guerguiev 2017]]) is one candidate mechanism for this constraint-discovery process.
- **Is "spikes are timed actions" implementable with single-cell-level credit assignment?** Brette's framing of action potentials as "potentials that produce actions" is conceptually appealing but doesn't immediately specify how learning operates. Hebbian plasticity is straightforward (local rule on co-occurring spikes). Hierarchical credit assignment is more contested: the supervised-learning reading ([[payeur-2020-burst-dependent-credit-assignment|Payeur 2020]], [[greedy-2022-single-phase-burstccn|Greedy 2022]]) needs an external target signal, which a subjective-physics reading would derive from sensorimotor mismatch. The algorithmic specification of learning over spike-action sequences in a closed loop is open.

## Principles vs Parameters & the Compressibility Question
*(New — added from Lillicrap & Kording 2019)*

- **Where on the compressibility axis does the brain actually sit?** [[lillicrap-kording-2019-understanding-neural-networks|Lillicrap & Kording (2019)]] place the brain on a continuum between tic-tac-toe (3 rules, fully compressible policy) and Go (>10¹⁰⁴⁸ games, no compact valuation) but do not estimate where. Empirical bounds on artificial-network compressibility (~100k parameters for ImageNet-scale recognition; similar sizes for MNIST that nonetheless resist human-readable compression) are one anchor. Whether biological brains are *more* compressible than ANNs at matched task scope (because of modularity, approximate linearity, or noise compressing the effective state space, all of which Lillicrap-Kording flag as candidates) or *less* compressible (because of cell-type heterogeneity, life-long memory, individual developmental trajectories) is unresolved.
- **What is the actual information content of the genome's brain-construction recipe?** ~6 × 10⁹ bits is the permissive upper bound; ~20k coding genes is the structural bound; non-coding regulatory elements may carry substantial additional information that has not been quantified. Tighter estimates would constrain how compressible the principles layer can be — and would in turn constrain hypotheses about which aspects of cortical structure are genetically specified vs. learned. The wiki's [[degeneracy|control-theoretic genes hypothesis]] depends on this question being answerable in a particular way (genome specifies dynamics + plasticity rules, not microscopic circuits).
- **Can the principles-vs-parameters split be made empirically operational?** The frame says target the recipe (architecture, plasticity, developmental program, learning signals), not the parameters (synaptic state, individual cell tuning). But many measurements straddle: a tuning curve is a parameter, but the *shape distribution* of tuning curves across a population is closer to a recipe-level property; a single dendrite's plateau-potential profile is a parameter, but the *plasticity rule* it implements is a principle. What measurement protocols cleanly target the recipe? The dendritic credit-assignment lineage ([[francioni-2026-vectorized-dendritic-signals|Francioni 2026]]) is the cleanest existing example — it tests a learning rule, not a representation — but generalizing the approach to other principles is open.
- **Does the [[computation-through-dynamics|CTD]] research program survive the principles-vs-parameters frame intact?** The strongest reading of CTD — that recovered low-dimensional dynamics *are* the explanation — is in tension with the frame, because the dynamics are properties of trained networks running on particular inputs. The CTD-compatible response is that dynamics are properties of the *recipe* (architecture + loss + training regime) rather than of any particular trained instantiation: if randomly initialized networks reliably develop the same dynamics when trained on the same task, the dynamics are recipe-level. Whether this defense holds across the full range of CTD findings — preparation as initial condition, output-null structure, rotational dynamics, line-attractor decisions — is an open empirical and conceptual question.
- **Are superhuman analyzers (future AI) the relevant unit of "understanding"?** [[lillicrap-kording-2019-understanding-neural-networks|Lillicrap & Kording]] raise the possibility that humans cannot understand systems performing at human-level complexity but that future AI systems might. If so, the bound on understanding is on the analyzer, not on the system, and the appropriate goal of neuroscience is *building tools that understand*, not directly understanding the brain ourselves. This connects to the methods-validation thread (Jonas-Kording 2017) — what does it look like to delegate understanding to an analytical apparatus, and what is the analogue of peer review or publication in such a workflow?
- **What does "fix the brain" look like under principles-only understanding?** The replace-with-synthetic test (memory and sensory neuroprosthetics) gestures at one operationalization. Under Lillicrap-Kording, successful prostheses should focus on substituting *principles* (architecture, plasticity dynamics) rather than reproducing *parameters* (specific synaptic state). Existing prosthetic work largely operates at the parameter level (hippocampal-prosthesis Berger work fits the input-output-of-a-region pattern). Whether principle-level prostheses (replace the plasticity rule, not the trained state) are even formulable is open.

Several of its synthesis claims (the nine-paradigm decomposition, the Kuhnian-frame mapping, the localized-crisis-signals diagnosis) have not been independently vetted against the philosophy-of-science or neuroscience literature for this wiki.
